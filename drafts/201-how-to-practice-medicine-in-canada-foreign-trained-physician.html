How to Practice Medicine in Canada as a Foreign-Trained Physician: Complete 2026 Guide

10 min read•2026-06-12

Becoming a licensed physician in Canada as a foreign-trained doctor is one of the most structured professional credential recognition processes in the world. It is also one of the most demanding. The pathway has clear stages, defined entry points, and documented requirements at every step. That structure is actually useful: it means you can build a plan, execute it systematically, and track your progress against known milestones.

Many international medical graduates (IMGs) have completed this journey and are now practicing in Canada. Some took five years. Some took seven. The range depends on where you start, which province you target, and how you sequence your immigration and licensing steps. What matters is that the path is well-defined and, with the right preparation, fully navigable.

This guide covers every major stage: credential verification, licensing exams, residency matching, provincial licensure, and the immigration status that lets you work legally while you complete the process.

The Four Stages at a Glance

Before diving into details, here is a map of the full pathway. Each stage feeds the next, and immigration planning should run in parallel, not after.

Stage What Happens Typical Timeline
1. MCC Credential Verification Medical Council of Canada verifies your degree and grants Source of Standing 3 to 6 months
2. MCC Licensing Exams MCCQE Part 1, NAC OSCE, and MCCQE Part 2 1 to 3 years depending on preparation and scheduling
3. Residency via CaRMS Apply to Canadian residency programs through the national match 1 to 2 years of preparation; residency itself is 2 to 5 years
4. Provincial Licensure Apply for a full or provisional license with your target college of physicians 1 to 3 months after residency or upon meeting provincial criteria

Immigration status runs alongside all four stages. You need to be legally permitted to study, train, or work in Canada at each step. Getting this wrong can interrupt your progression, so immigration planning should begin at the same time as your credential assessment, not after you pass your first exam.

Stage 1: MCC Credential Verification

The Medical Council of Canada (MCC) is the national body responsible for assessing whether your medical degree meets Canadian standards. Before you can sit any MCC exam, your credentials must be verified and you must receive a Source of Standing.

What the MCC evaluates

The MCC reviews your primary medical degree and verifies that the institution where you trained is listed in a recognized international directory, primarily the World Directory of Medical Schools. Canadian medical schools are automatically recognized. International schools must appear in the World Directory and meet minimum listing criteria.

The MCC does not evaluate your individual performance in medical school. It is not a merit-based assessment of your training quality. It is a verification that you hold a qualifying medical degree from a qualifying institution.

Documents you will need

The exact requirements vary based on your country of training, but typically include:

  • Primary source verification of your medical degree (requested directly from your university)
  • Official transcripts
  • Proof of medical registration or licensure in your home country (if applicable)
  • English or French language proficiency evidence, if required
  • Passport-quality identification

For Brazilian-trained physicians, the MCC generally requires your diploma from a Brazilian federal or state university recognized in the World Directory, plus verification of your CRM (Conselho Regional de Medicina) registration. Brazil has dozens of accredited medical schools listed in the World Directory, so graduation from a recognized institution is typically sufficient for credential purposes. The MCC does not require translation of your entire academic file, but any document not in English or French must be accompanied by a certified translation.

After verification

Once your credentials are verified, you receive a Source of Standing, which allows you to register for MCC exams. Keep this document. You will reference it repeatedly throughout the licensing process.

Stage 2: MCC Licensing Exams

There are three MCC exams in the standard IMG pathway. They are taken in sequence and each assesses a different dimension of medical competency.

MCCQE Part 1

The Medical Council of Canada Qualifying Examination Part 1 (MCCQE Part 1) is a computer-based, single-day examination. It consists of 210 questions in two formats: single best answer (multiple choice) and clinical decision-making (CDM) items.

The exam covers clinical knowledge across all major disciplines: medicine, surgery, pediatrics, obstetrics and gynecology, psychiatry, and preventive care. Questions are presented in a clinical vignette format and test your ability to diagnose, investigate, and manage patients in a Canadian clinical context.

The exam is offered year-round at Prometric testing centers around the world, including locations in Brazil and other countries where large IMG populations train. This means you can begin exam preparation and sit Part 1 while still abroad, which can save significant time if your immigration pathway allows it.

For current registration fees, exam dates, and testing center locations, check medicalcouncil.ca directly, as this information is updated regularly.

NAC OSCE

The National Assessment Collaboration Objective Structured Clinical Examination (NAC OSCE) is a hands-on clinical exam. It consists of 12 stations, each approximately 10 minutes long. At each station you encounter a standardized patient, a written scenario, or both, and are assessed on your clinical examination skills, communication, and patient management decisions.

The NAC OSCE is offered twice per year in Canada. Because it requires in-person attendance at a Canadian testing site, you must have valid immigration status that allows you to travel to Canada for the exam. This is a common point where immigration planning intersects directly with exam scheduling.

The exam assesses areas that Part 1 does not: your ability to communicate with patients and families in a Canadian clinical context, take a focused history, perform a relevant physical examination, and develop a management plan under observation.

MCCQE Part 2

The MCCQE Part 2 is an OSCE-format examination focused on clinical decision-making in realistic patient scenarios. It is required by most provincial licensing bodies before they will grant a full, independent practice license. Some provinces accept Part 1 results combined with NAC OSCE for provisional or supervised licensure, but full licensure almost universally requires Part 2.

Part 2 is offered less frequently than Part 1 and must also be taken in Canada. Most IMGs sit Part 2 during or after their residency training.

Stage 3: Residency Through CaRMS

Passing your MCC exams does not automatically give you a license to practice. In Canada, as in the United States and United Kingdom, physicians must complete an accredited residency program before they can practice independently. For most IMGs, accessing residency means going through the Canadian Resident Matching Service (CaRMS).

How the CaRMS match works

CaRMS runs a national matching process each year. Applicants submit applications to residency programs, programs rank applicants, and an algorithm matches applicants to programs in order of preference. There are two main iterations: the first and second iteration. IMGs who do not match in the first iteration can apply to unfilled positions in the second.

The IMG match rate has historically been lower than the Canadian medical graduate (CMG) rate. Published data from CaRMS shows that IMGs typically match at rates roughly half those of CMGs in the first iteration, though this varies significantly by specialty and province. Family medicine programs have historically had better IMG match rates than competitive surgical or specialty programs.

The practical implication: most IMGs who successfully enter Canadian residency do so in family medicine or general surgery rather than highly competitive subspecialties. Changing your target specialty after arriving in Canada, while possible, resets your timeline significantly.

IMG-friendly provinces

Not all provinces allocate the same number of residency seats to IMGs. Some provinces have historically been more accessible:

  • Manitoba and Saskatchewan have programs specifically designated for IMGs and actively recruit internationally trained physicians to address rural and underserved area shortages.
  • New Brunswick and other Atlantic provinces have made physician recruitment from abroad a provincial priority and often have IMG-stream residency positions.
  • Ontario has the largest absolute number of residency seats but also the highest competition. IMG match rates in Ontario are lower than in western and Atlantic provinces.

Quebec is a separate system. The College des medecins du Quebec (CMQ) runs its own evaluation and recognition process. IMGs who want to practice in Quebec must go through the CMQ route, which has different timelines, language requirements (French is essential), and procedures than the CaRMS national match.

What strengthens a CaRMS application

IMG applicants who match successfully typically have: strong MCC exam scores, Canadian clinical experience (through electives, observerships, or clinical assessment programs), strong reference letters from Canadian physicians, and a clear, coherent reason for choosing their target province and specialty. Work on these in parallel with your exam preparation.

Stage 4: Provincial Licensing

After completing residency, you apply to the provincial College of Physicians and Surgeons in the province where you intend to practice. Each college has its own requirements and licensing categories, but all require:

  • Completion of an accredited residency program
  • Satisfactory MCC exam results (including Part 2 for full licensure in most provinces)
  • Professional conduct review (no significant disciplinary history)
  • Proof of Canadian residency and work authorization

Key provincial colleges include:

  • CPSO (Ontario): College of Physicians and Surgeons of Ontario. Offers different registration classes including independent practice, academic, and restricted certificates.
  • CPSBC (British Columbia): College of Physicians and Surgeons of British Columbia. Has specific provisional licensing pathways for physicians completing additional requirements.
  • CPSA (Alberta): College of Physicians and Surgeons of Alberta. Alberta has a physician recruitment strategy that has made it a target province for some IMGs, particularly for rural and community practice.

Each college's website includes its current registration requirements, fees, and processing timelines. Requirements do change, and the information on the college's own website is always more current than any third-party summary.

Immigration Pathways: Running This in Parallel

Your immigration status determines what you can do and when. Many IMGs make the mistake of treating immigration as a separate, later concern. It is not. Immigration planning should begin at the same time as credential and exam planning.

NOC codes for physicians in Canada

Canada's National Occupational Classification system assigns specific codes to physician roles. These codes are used in immigration applications:

  • NOC 31102: General practitioners and family physicians
  • NOC 31100: Specialists in clinical and laboratory medicine
  • NOC 31101: Specialists in surgery (for surgical subspecialties)

Your immigration application will reference the NOC code that matches your intended practice.

Express Entry: Federal Skilled Worker

IMGs who already hold a medical degree and have sufficient exam results or international work experience may qualify for the Federal Skilled Worker Program through Express Entry. This is a points-based system (Comprehensive Ranking System, or CRS) that considers your age, education, language proficiency, and work experience.

A strong CRS score can lead to a permanent residence invitation without requiring a job offer. However, most physicians applying through Express Entry also benefit from a provincial nomination, which significantly boosts CRS points.

For a full explanation of how Express Entry works, see our Express Entry guide.

Provincial Nominee Programs for healthcare workers

Several provinces have specific streams within their Provincial Nominee Programs (PNPs) designed to attract healthcare workers, including physicians. These streams often have:

  • Lower CRS score thresholds than the federal pool
  • Faster processing for nominees who commit to practicing in underserved areas
  • Connection to provincial physician recruitment initiatives

Saskatchewan, Manitoba, Nova Scotia, and New Brunswick have been particularly active in nominating healthcare workers. If you are targeting one of these provinces for residency anyway, a provincial nomination can work in parallel with your CaRMS application.

For more on how permanent residence pathways work, see our permanent residence guide.

LMIA-based work permits

If you receive a residency offer, that offer can support a work permit application. Residency programs are typically LMIA-exempt under intra-company or international agreement provisions, but some positions require a Labour Market Impact Assessment (LMIA). Your residency program administrator will clarify which applies.

For physicians who match into a residency position, a work permit is the most common immigration status during training. If you are already in Canada on a different permit (student, visitor, spousal), you will need to transition to an appropriate work authorization before you can be paid as a resident physician.

Realistic Timeline: What to Expect

The honest answer is that this pathway is measured in years, not months. Here are two realistic scenarios:

Starting from abroad with no Canadian exams:

  • MCC verification: 3 to 6 months
  • MCCQE Part 1 preparation and sitting: 12 to 18 months
  • NAC OSCE preparation and sitting: 6 to 12 months after Part 1
  • CaRMS application, match, and start of residency: 6 to 18 months after NAC OSCE
  • Residency duration: 2 years (family medicine) to 5+ years (subspecialty)
  • Full licensure: 1 to 3 months after residency completion

Total from abroad: roughly 5 to 8 years to independent practice

Starting from within Canada with Part 1 already passed:

  • NAC OSCE: 6 months preparation if you are well-organized
  • CaRMS match: next available cycle
  • Residency: 2 to 5 years
  • Licensure: 1 to 3 months after

Total from this starting point: roughly 3 to 6 years to independent practice

These timelines assume you pass each exam on the first attempt and match into residency in your first CaRMS iteration. Many IMGs need additional attempts at one or more stages, which extends the timeline.

First Steps to Take Right Now

If you are a foreign-trained physician reading this and wondering where to start, here is a practical sequence:

  1. Verify your medical school is in the World Directory of Medical Schools. Go to wdoms.org and search your institution. If it is not listed, you will need legal and academic guidance before proceeding.
  2. Create an MCC account and apply for Source of Standing. Do this before you do anything else. The verification process takes months, and you cannot register for exams without it.
  3. Begin MCCQE Part 1 preparation. This exam is the entry gate and can be sat outside Canada. Start here.
  4. Research your target province. Your choice of province affects which residency programs you can access, which PNP streams apply, and which provincial college you will license with. Make this decision deliberately.
  5. Get clarity on your immigration status. Whether you are applying from abroad, studying in Canada, or working on a permit, your current status affects what you can do and when. Consult a licensed immigration professional to map this out before you commit to a timeline.
