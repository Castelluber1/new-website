Canada Study Permit Processing Time for Brazilians in 2026

7 min read•2026-05-20

For Brazilians applying from outside Canada, the current IRCC processing time for a study permit is 5 weeks. That figure is specific to Brazil, as of May 13, 2026, and it is the number you should be building your timeline around right now. Not the global average, not what a friend heard last year. Five weeks for Brazil, from outside Canada, today.

That said, five weeks on the IRCC tool does not mean five weeks from when you hit submit to when a permit arrives at your address. There are steps before and after the IRCC clock that Brazilian applicants routinely underestimate. This article covers what those steps are, what the 5-week figure actually measures, and the specific requirements that apply to Brazilians: biometrics, the Provincial Attestation Letter, proof of funds, and what IRCC is looking for in your file specifically.

How to check your processing time on the IRCC tool

IRCC publishes country-specific processing times for every permit category through an official online tool. Here is how to pull the Brazil figure yourself:

  1. Go to the IRCC Processing Times Tool.
  2. Under "What are you applying for," select Temporary residence (visiting, studying, working).
  3. Select Study permit (from outside Canada).
  4. Under country, select Brazil.
  5. Click Get processing time. The result as of May 13, 2026: 5 weeks.

IRCC updates this tool weekly. The number can shift in either direction depending on application volumes and staffing. Check it again the week you plan to submit, because the figure you see today may not be the figure that applies when your file lands.

What "5 weeks" actually means

The IRCC processing time is not an average and it is not a guarantee. It represents the time it took IRCC to finalize 80% of applications in the same category over a recent historical window. That means one in five applicants will wait longer, sometimes significantly longer.

Two other things shape what that number means in practice:

The clock starts on a complete application. IRCC does not start counting the moment you upload your documents. The processing time is measured from the point when your application is considered administratively complete: fees paid, biometrics received, all required documents present and legible, and any mandatory letters included (more on the PAL below). A missing document or an expired passport scan does not pause your clock. It effectively restarts it.

Five weeks does not cover the full timeline. Before IRCC starts the clock, you need to gather and prepare your documents, collect a Provincial Attestation Letter from your school, and give biometrics. After IRCC issues a decision, there may be processing at the port of entry. The realistic end-to-end timeline for a Brazilian applicant, from deciding to apply to being ready to travel, is closer to 10 to 14 weeks when biometrics and document preparation are included.

For planning purposes, build your submission date around the program start, not around the published 5 weeks.

When to submit for September 2026 and January 2027 intakes

Most Brazilian students are targeting one of two standard Canadian academic intakes: September or January. Here is what the timeline looks like for each.

September 2026 start: Classes typically begin in the first week of September. To give yourself a full buffer for biometrics, document review, and any IRCC delays, aim to submit your complete application by mid-June 2026. That leaves roughly 10 to 12 weeks before your program begins. Submitting in late June is possible but cuts the margin significantly. Submitting in July for a September start is a risk most advisors would not recommend without strong reason.

January 2027 start: Classes typically begin in early January. Target a submission date of early October 2026. That gives you the same 10 to 12 week window with room for the biometrics appointment, document gathering, and the standard IRCC processing period.

If you are reading this in late May or June and targeting September 2026, move quickly. The window is not closed, but it is narrowing.

The PAL requirement for Brazilian students

Since January 2024, most study permit applicants from outside Canada are required to include a Provincial Attestation Letter (PAL) with their application. This requirement applies to Brazilian applicants and is one of the most common reasons for refusals that could have been avoided.

A PAL is a letter issued by the Canadian province or territory where your school is located, confirming that the province supports your enrollment within its study permit allocation for the year. You do not apply for it directly. Your school, which must be a Designated Learning Institution (DLI), applies for the attestation on your behalf. When it is issued, the school sends it to you, and you include it in your IRCC application package.

What this means practically:

  • Contact your DLI admissions office and confirm they will issue you a PAL and when.
  • Do not submit your study permit application without it. Applications missing a PAL are refused, not returned for correction.
  • PAL processing timelines vary by province. Ontario and British Columbia currently have different PAL systems and timelines. Ask your school early.
  • Some categories are exempt from the PAL requirement, including master's and doctoral students at most institutions, and certain short-term programs. Ask your DLI whether your program is exempt before assuming it is.

Biometrics for Brazilians: add approximately 30 days

Brazilian applicants who have not previously given biometrics for a Canadian application will receive a Biometrics Instruction Letter (BIL) after submitting. You then have 30 days to attend a Visa Application Centre (VAC) to give fingerprints and a photo.

IRCC does not process your application beyond a certain point until biometrics are received. In practice, this means biometrics can add 2 to 4 weeks to your total timeline depending on VAC availability.

VAC locations in Brazil for Canadian biometrics:

  • São Paulo
  • Rio de Janeiro
  • Brasília
  • Curitiba
  • Recife

If you live in another city, plan for the travel. VAC appointments can be booked in advance, and availability varies by location and season. Do not wait for the BIL to check appointment availability at your nearest VAC. Check now, book as soon as you receive the letter, and factor transit time into your overall schedule.

Biometrics collected for a previous Canadian application (visitor visa, for example) remain valid for 10 years. If you have given biometrics before and that window has not expired, you will not need to give them again.

What IRCC looks at for Brazilian applicants specifically

A study permit is not automatic. An IRCC officer reviews your file and must be satisfied on several points. For Brazilian applicants, these are the four areas that carry the most weight:

Proof of funds. You need to demonstrate enough money to cover tuition plus living costs for the duration of your program. The standard IRCC threshold for the first year outside Quebec is CAD $22,895 for living expenses, on top of tuition. The money should be in your account or a sponsor's account, with at least 3 to 6 months of consistent bank history. Large deposits that appeared recently without a clear explanation raise questions.

Ties to Brazil. The officer must believe you will return home after your studies. Ties include family members in Brazil, property ownership, an employment relationship, a business, or any other documented connection to your life in Brazil. For younger applicants who are recent graduates with no property and no employment history, this section requires deliberate attention. The absence of ties is one of the most common reasons Brazilian study permits are refused.

Study plan. Your application should include a written statement explaining why you chose this specific program at this specific school and how it connects to your educational background and career goals in Brazil. The plan should make logical sense. A marketing professional applying for an accounting program with no explanation looks inconsistent. A nursing graduate applying for a healthcare management certificate that builds on their clinical experience looks coherent.

Acceptance letter from a Designated Learning Institution. Your letter of acceptance must come from a DLI. You can confirm DLI status for any school through the official IRCC DLI list. The acceptance letter must reflect the program you are applying to study.

Common reasons Brazilians get refused

Study permit refusals for Brazilian nationals tend to cluster around a few predictable issues. Understanding them before you apply is significantly more useful than discovering them after.

Weak ties to Brazil. This is the most common reason. Officers assess whether you are genuinely a student planning to return home or whether your real intention is to remain in Canada. Young applicants with no property, no employment, no family dependents, and no documented plan for life after graduation are vulnerable to this. You cannot invent ties, but you can document the ones you have thoroughly and write a study plan that articulates clearly how this Canadian credential serves a specific career goal in Brazil.

Vague or unconvincing study plan. A one-paragraph generic statement about wanting to improve your career does not satisfy the officer. The plan needs to explain the logic: your current credentials, why this specific program fills a specific gap, why Canada and this institution, and what you intend to do with the credential when you return. Specificity matters.

Funds that appeared recently. Bank statements where a large sum arrived one or two months before the application, without documentation explaining the source, read as artificially staged. If you are relying on a family sponsor, include their bank statements, their employment letter, and a signed sponsor declaration. The source of the money needs to be traceable.

Inconsistency between program and background. Applying for a program that has no connection to your previous education or work history without explaining the transition raises questions about whether your stated purpose is genuine. If you are making a legitimate career change, explain it. If the program is not obviously connected to your background, make the connection explicit.

Missing PAL. Since 2024, applications without a Provincial Attestation Letter where one is required are refused outright. This is preventable. Confirm with your school before submitting.

The bottom line

The current IRCC processing time for a Brazilian study permit applicant from outside Canada is 5 weeks, as of May 13, 2026. That number applies to complete applications only, and it covers 80% of cases. The full timeline from decision-to-apply to ready-to-travel is closer to 10 to 14 weeks once you add biometrics, document preparation, and the PAL from your school.

For a September 2026 start, submit by mid-June. For a January 2027 start, submit by early October. Give your DLI enough time to issue the PAL before you submit. Do not submit without it.
