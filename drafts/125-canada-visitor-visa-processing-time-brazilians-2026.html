Canada Visitor Visa Processing Time for Brazilians in 2026

7 min read•2026-05-20

If you are a Brazilian citizen applying for a Canadian visitor visa in 2026, IRCC's current processing time for applications from Brazil is 29 days. That figure comes directly from the official IRCC tool, last checked May 13, 2026. This article explains what that number means, what Brazilians specifically need to prepare, and how to build a realistic timeline around your trip plans.

Canada does not offer visa-free entry to Brazilian passport holders. Whether you are visiting family in Toronto, planning a trip through British Columbia, attending a wedding in Montreal, or spending a ski holiday in Whistler, you need a Temporary Resident Visa (TRV) before you board your flight. The good news is that IRCC has been processing Brazilian applications within a predictable window in 2026.

How to Check the Current Processing Time

Processing times are country-specific and IRCC updates them regularly. The number above reflects data from May 13, 2026. By the time you read this, the figure may have changed. The only reliable source is the official IRCC tool, and checking it takes under a minute.

Here are the exact steps:

  1. Go to the IRCC processing times tool.
  2. Under "What type of application is it?", select Temporary residence (visiting, studying, working).
  3. Under "What application are you checking?", select Visitor visa (from outside Canada).
  4. Under "What country are you applying from?", select Brazil.
  5. Click "Get processing time" to see the current estimate.

The result: 29 days for visitor visa applications from Brazil as of May 13, 2026.

What "29 Days" Actually Means

The published processing time is not a deadline and it is not a guarantee. IRCC calculates it based on how long it took to process 80% of complete applications from Brazil in a recent reference period. That means one in five Brazilian applicants will wait longer than 29 days, sometimes considerably longer.

There are three things that determine which side of that line your application lands on.

Your application must be complete. The 29-day clock applies only to applications that arrive with all required documents, fully completed forms, and no inconsistencies. If IRCC sends you a request for additional documents and your file pauses while you gather and upload them, that waiting time is not counted in the published figure. Your actual processing time grows by however long you take to respond.

The figure is Brazil-specific. Applicants from other countries see different numbers in the same tool. The 29-day estimate reflects the volume of Brazilian applications, the processing centres that handle them, historical approval rates, and any bilateral verification requirements that apply to Brazilian nationals. Do not rely on figures you see quoted for other nationalities.

Officer workload and file complexity matter. Even complete applications from the same country do not all move at the same speed. A straightforward application with clear finances, strong ties to Brazil, and a well-documented purpose of visit tends to move faster than one that requires additional scrutiny. If something in your file prompts a closer look, your timeline extends accordingly.

Think of 29 days as the realistic baseline for a clean, well-prepared Brazilian application. Use it as the minimum, not the target, when planning your trip.

Biometrics: The Step Most Brazilians Underestimate

Most Brazilian applicants applying for a Canadian visa for the first time, or for the first time in more than 10 years, will need to provide biometrics. This is a separate step that happens after you submit your online application, and it adds time to your total timeline.

Here is how it works. Shortly after submitting your application online, you will receive a Biometric Instruction Letter (BIL) from IRCC. You then have 30 days to attend an appointment at a Visa Application Centre (VAC) in Brazil to provide your fingerprints and photo.

VAC locations currently operating in Brazil include major cities such as São Paulo, Rio de Janeiro, Brasília, Curitiba, and Recife. Appointment availability varies. In São Paulo especially, slots can fill up days in advance during busy periods. Book your appointment as soon as the instruction letter arrives.

Once biometrics are submitted, your application continues processing. The 29-day IRCC estimate begins after IRCC receives your biometrics, though in practice the two steps can overlap. The practical implication: your total timeline from submission to decision is closer to 6 to 8 weeks when biometrics are included, not just 29 days.

If you have already given biometrics to IRCC within the last 10 years, you will not need to provide them again. Check the date of your previous submission to confirm.

Building Your Trip Timeline

If you are planning a trip to Canada from Brazil in 2026, here is a practical timeline framework to work from.

Add together: biometrics appointment time (up to 2 weeks to get an appointment and attend), biometrics processing (a few days to a week after your appointment), IRCC visa review (29 days from when biometrics are received), and a buffer for any document requests or delays (1 to 2 weeks). The total comes to roughly 8 to 10 weeks from the day you submit your application.

Working backwards: if you want to be in Canada on a specific date, submit your visitor visa application at least 10 weeks in advance. For high-travel seasons, including summer (July and August) when many Brazilian families visit relatives in cities like Toronto and Vancouver, and December holidays, apply even earlier. VAC appointment availability and IRCC volumes both increase during those periods.

Do not book non-refundable flights or accommodations before your visa is approved. The 29-day figure is an average, not a ceiling, and an application under review does not guarantee approval.

Common Reasons Canadian Visitor Visa Applications from Brazil Are Refused

Brazil has a substantial volume of visitor visa applications to Canada each year. Officers reviewing Brazilian applications are experienced and follow consistent criteria. Understanding the most common refusal reasons helps you address them before submission rather than after a refusal.

Ties to Brazil not clearly demonstrated. This is the most frequent cause of refusal for Brazilian applicants. The officer needs to believe you will leave Canada when your authorized stay ends. That means your application must show compelling reasons to return home: employment, a business, family members who depend on you, property, financial obligations, or ongoing education. A vague statement of intent is not enough.

Insufficient financial documentation. You need to show you can support yourself during your stay in Canada without working illegally. Bank statements with recent large deposits, income that does not match your employment letter, or financial records that are unclear raise credibility concerns. Consistent, well-organized financial documentation strengthens your application significantly.

Purpose of visit not clearly documented. Officers want to understand specifically why you are going and what you will do there. If you are visiting family in Canada, an invitation letter from the relative, proof of their status in Canada, and a clear itinerary all help. If you are attending a specific event, document it. If you are a tourist planning to visit Vancouver, Banff, or Niagara Falls, show that the trip is concrete and purposeful.

Thin or inconsistent travel history. Applicants with limited international travel history face more scrutiny. If you have not traveled outside Brazil much, your application needs to work harder to compensate through strong ties to home and a clearly documented trip purpose.

Previous Canadian or other visa refusals not disclosed. Canadian immigration forms ask directly about prior refusals. Omitting or misrepresenting this history is treated as misrepresentation, which is far more serious than the original refusal and can result in a multi-year ban. Always disclose prior refusals accurately and explain what has changed since then.
