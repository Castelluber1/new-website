Canada PR Card Renewal: Step-by-Step Guide for Permanent Residents

10 min read•2026-05-18

Your Permanent Resident (PR) card is one of the most important documents you hold as a Canadian permanent resident. It proves your status in Canada when you re-enter the country after traveling abroad, and it's the standard ID used by employers, financial institutions, and government agencies to confirm your permanent resident status.

When it expires — or before it does — renewal is straightforward, but the residency obligation check built into the process catches many PRs off guard. This guide explains when and how to renew, what IRCC checks during renewal, and what to do if you haven't met the residency requirement.

What Is the PR Card and When Does It Expire?

The PR card is a wallet-sized document issued to all permanent residents of Canada. It includes your photo, name, date of birth, and the expiry date of the card (not the expiry of your PR status — your status itself does not expire, only the card).

Standard validity: 5 years for adults. 1 year for PRs under 14 (who will need to renew as a child and then as an adult). First-time PR cards may be issued for a shorter period in some cases.

An expired PR card does not affect your PR status. You remain a permanent resident. However:

  • You cannot re-enter Canada from abroad without a valid PR card (or a PRTD — see below)
  • You cannot use an expired PR card as ID for employment, banking, or government purposes
  • Airlines will typically not board you on a flight to Canada with an expired PR card

The Residency Obligation

Before IRCC issues a new PR card, they check whether you've met the residency obligation: you must have been physically present in Canada for at least 730 days (2 years) during the previous 5-year period.

This is the critical check. If you've spent significant time outside Canada — for work, family, travel, or any other reason — you may not have met the 730-day requirement. IRCC does not rubber-stamp renewals.

What counts toward the 730 days:

  • Days physically present in Canada
  • Days outside Canada accompanying a Canadian citizen spouse or common-law partner
  • Days outside Canada as a full-time employee of a Canadian business or the federal/provincial government

What does NOT count:

  • Days outside Canada on vacation
  • Days outside Canada for personal reasons unrelated to the categories above
  • Employment by a foreign company, even if that company has Canadian operations

How IRCC verifies: When you submit your PR card renewal, you disclose your travel history for the past 5 years. IRCC cross-references this with CBSA entry records and passport stamps.

Do You Meet the 730-Day Requirement?

Before applying, count your days:

  1. Get a copy of your travel history — review your passport stamps and create a log of dates in and out of Canada
  2. Request your CBSA border crossing records if needed — you can do this through an ATIP request or through a Service Canada inquiry
  3. If you have qualifying days abroad (accompanying Canadian citizen spouse, or working for a Canadian employer), gather documentation for those days

If your count shows you have at least 730 days in Canada during the past 5 years, you can proceed confidently with the renewal.

If you're close to the threshold (e.g., 700-730 days), it may be worth waiting until you have more days accumulated before applying.

If you haven't met the 730-day requirement, do not simply submit the renewal without a plan. See the section below on what to do.

How to Apply for PR Card Renewal

Step 1: Gather Your Documents

  • Completed application forms: IMM 5444 (Application to Renew a Permanent Resident Card)
  • Current PR card (even if expired — or a statutory declaration explaining why you don't have it)
  • Passport (valid — all pages showing travel history for the past 5 years)
  • Travel history details: Entry/exit dates and countries visited for the past 5 years
  • Two photos meeting IRCC specifications (same as passport photo standards)
  • Proof of supporting documents (if claiming days outside Canada accompanying a Canadian citizen or working for a Canadian employer)

Step 2: Pay the Application Fee

The fee for PR card renewal is $50 CAD. Pay online through the IRCC portal.

Step 3: Submit Online

PR card renewal applications are submitted online through your IRCC secure account (MyAccount). Paper submissions are no longer accepted for most applicants.

Step 4: Wait for Processing

Processing times vary, but IRCC targets processing within a few months. Current processing times are published on the IRCC website — check before applying to set expectations.

While your renewal is pending, you can continue living and working in Canada. If you need to travel internationally while waiting, you must either wait for the new card or obtain a PRTD (see below).

Step 5: Receive Your New Card

IRCC will mail your new PR card to your address in Canada. Ensure your address on file is current.

Traveling While Your Renewal Is Pending: The PRTD

If your PR card has expired and you need to travel outside Canada while a renewal is pending (or even before applying), you cannot use your expired card to re-enter. You'll need a Permanent Resident Travel Document (PRTD).

What is a PRTD? A PRTD is a single-use travel document that allows you to board a flight to Canada. It's issued by a Canadian visa post abroad and is only available if you are outside Canada when you apply.

PRTD process:

  1. Apply online through IRCC or at the nearest Canadian Embassy or Visa Application Centre
  2. Provide evidence of PR status (old PR card, COPR, other documents)
  3. Demonstrate you meet the residency obligation (or qualify for an exemption)
  4. Pay the $50 CAD fee

Processing: Can take 1–6+ weeks depending on the visa post. Apply well in advance if you know you'll need to return.

Important: If IRCC determines during the PRTD application that you haven't met the residency obligation, they can refuse the PRTD and begin the process of finding you in breach of the residency obligation — which can lead to a referral for a departure order. The PRTD process has the same residency obligation check built in.

What If You Haven't Met the 730-Day Requirement?

If you've calculated your days and concluded you haven't met the 730-day threshold, you have options:

Option 1: Accumulate Enough Days and Then Apply

If you're currently in Canada and close to the threshold, simply stay until you've accumulated 730 days, then apply for renewal.

Option 2: Humanitarian and Compassionate (H&C) Grounds

If you genuinely haven't met the residency obligation, you can disclose this in your renewal application and request consideration on H&C grounds. IRCC can still renew your card (or issue you a PRTD) if:

  • You were outside Canada for reasons beyond your control (serious medical condition, family emergency, deployment)
  • Your ties to Canada are significant
  • The impact of losing PR status would be disproportionate to the circumstances

H&C is discretionary — there is no guarantee. The strength of your case depends on why you were outside Canada and the strength of your Canadian establishment.

Option 3: Prepare for a Possible Loss of Status Process

If your breach is significant — you've been outside Canada for 3-4+ years without qualifying days — you may be referred to the Immigration Division, which can issue a departure order. This is not automatic, and you have the right to a hearing. But the risk is real.

In this situation, consulting with an immigration lawyer or RCIC before applying is strongly recommended. Do not submit an application that will reveal a significant residency breach without professional advice.

Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

1. Waiting until the card has expired: Renewals can take months. Apply 9-12 months before expiry so you have a valid card for travel.

2. Underestimating time outside Canada: Many PRs are surprised when they calculate their days. Be precise — use actual dates, not estimates.

3. Not accounting for qualifying days abroad: If you traveled for a Canadian employer or accompanied a Canadian citizen spouse, those days count. Document them.

4. Not updating your address: IRCC mails the new card. If you've moved, update your address in your IRCC account before the card is mailed.

5. Applying while abroad: The renewal application requires you to be in Canada. If your card has expired and you're outside Canada, you need a PRTD to return first.

Frequently Asked Questions

My PR card expired 3 years ago. Am I still a permanent resident? Yes — your PR status doesn't expire. But you should check whether you've met the residency obligation, and you should be aware that you'll need a PRTD to return to Canada if you're currently abroad.

Can I work in Canada with an expired PR card? Technically yes — your PR status authorizes you to work. But many employers require a valid PR card as proof of status. You may face practical difficulties without a valid card.

My PR card says "see notes" — what does that mean? "See notes" on a PR card typically refers to conditions or additional information in IRCC's system. It's not common. If you have this on your card, contact IRCC for clarification before applying for renewal.

How do I track my application status? Log into your IRCC secure account. Your application status is updated as it moves through processing stages.

Conclusion

PR card renewal is one of the more routine immigration tasks — but the residency obligation check makes it critical to count your days carefully before applying. Most permanent residents who have genuinely been living in Canada meet the threshold easily. Those who have spent significant time abroad need to plan more carefully.

Renew early, count your days honestly, and ensure your travel documentation is complete before submitting. If there's any doubt about whether you've met the residency obligation, consult an immigration professional before applying — it's much easier to plan proactively than to respond to a residency breach finding.
