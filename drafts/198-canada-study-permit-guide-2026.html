Canada Study Permit 2026: Complete Guide for International Students

10 min read•2026-05-18

Canada is one of the top destinations for international students worldwide, home to globally ranked universities, accessible post-graduation work opportunities, and a clear pathway to permanent residence for graduates who want to stay. But before any of that happens, you need a study permit.

This guide covers everything you need to know about the Canada study permit: who needs one, the eligibility requirements, how to apply, what the new Student Direct Stream means for your timeline, and how your study permit connects to your long-term immigration options.

What Is a Study Permit?

A study permit is a document issued by IRCC that authorizes a foreign national to study at a Designated Learning Institution (DLI) in Canada. A DLI is any Canadian school approved by provincial or territorial governments to host international students.

Most universities, colleges, and many private career colleges in Canada are DLIs. Trade schools, flight academies, and certain language schools may or may not be DLIs, always verify before applying.

A study permit is not a visa. If you need a visa to enter Canada (citizens of most countries do), you also need a Temporary Resident Visa (TRV) or an Electronic Travel Authorization (eTA) to enter Canada in addition to your study permit. When you apply for a study permit, IRCC automatically processes the TRV or eTA alongside it if needed.

Who Needs a Study Permit?

Most foreign nationals studying in Canada for more than 6 months need a study permit. Exceptions:

  • Short programs under 6 months in duration where you don't plan to extend your stay
  • Exchange students at certain NAFTA/CUSMA designated programs (specific exceptions apply)
  • Children of certain foreign workers under specific categories

If your program is longer than 6 months, which includes virtually all university degrees, college diplomas, and many professional certificate programs, you need a study permit.

Eligibility Requirements

To be eligible for a Canadian study permit, you must:

  1. Be accepted at a Designated Learning Institution (DLI), you need a valid Letter of Acceptance (LOA) before applying

  2. Demonstrate financial sufficiency: IRCC requires proof you can pay: - Your first year's tuition - Living expenses for yourself (and family members coming with you): ~$10,000 CAD per year (outside Quebec) or ~$11,000 (Quebec) - Return transportation costs

  3. Have no criminal record that would make you inadmissible to Canada

  4. Be in good health (a medical exam may be required depending on your country of origin and program type, e.g., healthcare programs)

  5. Satisfy the officer that you will leave Canada at the end of your authorized stay, this is the "ties to home country" assessment (see below)

  6. Agree to comply with the conditions of a study permit (remain a full-time student, notify IRCC of changes in program, etc.)

The "Ties to Home Country" Requirement and How IRCC Assesses It

One of the primary reasons study permit applications are refused is the officer's doubt that the applicant will leave Canada at the end of their authorized stay. IRCC is particularly attentive to this for applicants from certain countries.

What demonstrates ties:

  • Owned property or business interests in your home country
  • Strong family ties (spouse, children, parents who are staying behind)
  • Employment history and a job to return to
  • Financial assets in your home country
  • Community ties (civic, religious, social organizations)

What raises concerns:

  • Being young and single with no dependents at home
  • No employment or business history
  • Previously overstayed a visa anywhere
  • Family already in Canada on various permits

This doesn't mean young single students can't get study permits, millions do. But it's important to present your file in a way that addresses this concern proactively in your Statement of Purpose.

How to Apply: Step by Step

Step 1: Get a Letter of Acceptance

You cannot apply for a study permit without an LOA from a DLI. Apply to your school first. Once you receive an LOA, you can begin the permit application.

Step 2: Create an IRCC Online Account

Study permit applications are submitted through the IRCC online portal (MyAccount). Create an account if you don't have one.

Step 3: Fill Out the Application Forms

The core forms are the IMM 1294 (Application for a Study Permit) and a family information form (IMM 5707 or similar). Complete all sections accurately, misrepresentation is grounds for refusal and a potential bar.

Step 4: Submit Documents and Pay Fees

Upload your documents and pay the application fee:

  • Study permit: $150 CAD
  • Biometrics: $85 CAD (per person; family: $170 CAD)

Step 5: Provide Biometrics (if required)

After submitting your application, you may receive a biometrics request. You must visit a Visa Application Centre (VAC) in your country to provide fingerprints and a photo. Biometrics are valid for 10 years once given, if you've previously given biometrics for a Canadian application, you may not need to give them again.

Step 6: Medical Exam (if required)

Certain nationalities and program types (healthcare, working with children) require a medical exam by an IRCC-designated physician. Check the IRCC website to see if your country requires a medical exam before applying.

Step 7: Wait for a Decision

Processing times vary significantly by country of application. The Student Direct Stream (SDS) offers faster processing for eligible countries (see below).

Student Direct Stream (SDS): Faster Processing

The Student Direct Stream is a faster processing track for study permit applicants from specific countries who meet additional requirements. As of 2026, eligible countries include India, China, Philippines, Vietnam, Senegal, Cameroon, Pakistan, Morocco, Antigua and Barbuda, and several others. The list has expanded over time, verify the current list at IRCC.

SDS requirements (in addition to standard eligibility):

  • Paid your first year's tuition in full before applying
  • Have a valid GIC (Guaranteed Investment Certificate) of at least $20,635 CAD held by a participating Canadian financial institution
  • Language test results above minimum thresholds (IELTS 6.0 in each band, or equivalent)
  • You are applying from outside Canada (not for a change of conditions inside Canada)
  • Medical exam completed before applying (if required)

If you meet all SDS requirements, IRCC targets a 20-business-day processing time, significantly faster than the standard stream.

Conditions of a Study Permit

Once in Canada on a study permit, you must:

  • Be enrolled at a DLI
  • Study at the institution and in the program stated in your permit (or notify IRCC if you change schools/programs)
  • Maintain your enrollment as a full-time student (unless it's your final semester)
  • Leave Canada when your permit expires unless you've applied for an extension, a PGWP, or another status

If you take a leave of absence from your studies, you may be violating your permit conditions, consult with your school's international student office before taking any leave longer than 150 days.

Working While Studying

Study permit holders in full-time programs at DLIs can generally work:

  • On campus: Unlimited hours
  • Off campus: Up to 24 hours per week during academic sessions; full-time during scheduled breaks (this was temporarily increased from 20h, verify current rule)

After graduation, the Post-Graduation Work Permit (PGWP) allows you to work in Canada for up to 3 years, the single most important post-study immigration pathway for most international students.

Study Permit and the Path to Permanent Residence

The typical pathway from study permit to PR:

  1. Study permit → complete a qualifying program at a DLI
  2. PGWP → work in Canada for up to 3 years
  3. Accumulate 12 months of Canadian work experience (TEER 0-3 occupation)
  4. Canadian Experience Class (CEC) → apply for PR through Express Entry

This is the most common pathway for international graduates in skilled occupations. The combination of Canadian education and Canadian work experience produces strong CRS scores.

Additional pathways:

  • Provincial Nominee Programs, many provinces have dedicated streams for international graduates who studied or worked in the province
  • Rural and Northern Immigration Pilot (RNIP), for graduates in eligible communities
  • Atlantic Immigration Program (AIP), for graduates who studied or worked in Atlantic Canada

Extending Your Study Permit

If your program extends beyond your permit's expiry, you must apply to extend it before it expires. Apply through your IRCC online account. While your extension is pending, you maintain maintained status (implied status), you can continue studying as if your permit were still valid while waiting for the decision.

If your permit expires and you haven't applied for an extension, you fall out of status. You can apply for restoration of status within 90 days, but this adds cost, processing time, and uncertainty.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I apply for a study permit if I've been refused before? Yes, a previous refusal doesn't bar you from applying again. However, you must disclose prior refusals in your new application, and you should address the reason for the previous refusal in your application.

Can my spouse/family come with me? Yes. Your spouse may be eligible for an open spousal work permit while you study (if you're in an eligible program, generally degree-level at a university or certain college programs). Dependent children can enroll in Canadian schools.

Do I need private health insurance? Yes, you are not automatically covered by provincial health insurance from day one. Most universities charge a mandatory international student health insurance fee. Some provinces now cover international students under their health plan after a waiting period, verify the rules in your province.

Can I start studying before my study permit arrives? If you already have a valid study permit and applied for a new one, yes, implied status allows you to continue studying. If this is your first study permit, you must wait until you receive it and are inside Canada to begin.

Conclusion

A Canadian study permit is the gateway to one of the world's best international education experiences, and in many cases, the first step toward Canadian permanent residence. The process is document-heavy but well-defined, and for applicants who understand what IRCC is looking for, refusals are preventable.

The keys are: a genuine acceptance at a real DLI, strong financial documentation, a compelling Statement of Purpose that addresses your ties to home and your plan after graduation, and (for eligible countries) using the Student Direct Stream for faster processing.
