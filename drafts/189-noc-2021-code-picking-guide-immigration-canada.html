How to Pick the Right NOC 2021 Code for Canadian Immigration in 2026

10 min read•2026-05-17

Picking the wrong National Occupational Classification (NOC) code is one of the fastest ways to get a Canadian immigration application refused. It looks like a simple five-digit number on the form, but that number quietly decides your TEER level, whether you qualify for Express Entry, how many CRS points you get for foreign work experience, and whether an officer believes your work history matches what you claim it does.

This guide walks you through the NOC 2021 system the way our office at UP Immigration Consulting walks clients through it before we ever touch an application: structure first, then duties, then evidence, then edge cases. If you finish this article and still feel unsure about your code, that uncertainty is the message, book a consultation before you submit anything binding.

What the NOC 2021 code actually is

NOC 2021 is the classification system Statistics Canada and Employment and Social Development Canada (ESDC) use to describe every job in the Canadian labour market. Immigration, Refugees and Citizenship Canada (IRCC) adopted it for all economic immigration programs on November 16, 2022, replacing the older NOC 2016 four-digit format.

Every occupation now has a 5-digit code. The structure is precise:

  • First digit = TEER level (Training, Education, Experience and Responsibilities). TEER runs from 0 to 5.
  • Digits 2 to 5 = the specific occupation within that TEER and broad category.

So a code like 21231 (software engineers) tells an IRCC officer two things instantly: TEER 1 (degree-level work, equivalent to the old NOC Skill Level A), and a specific occupation inside the computing and information systems group.

TEER levels at a glance

TEER Typical occupations Old NOC 2016 equivalent
0 Management roles, restaurant managers, construction managers, financial managers Skill Type 0
1 Roles needing a university degree, software engineers, RCICs, physicians, lawyers Skill Level A
2 Roles needing a college diploma or 2+ years apprenticeship, or supervisory roles, computer network technicians, dental hygienists, plumbers (supervisor) Skill Level B (part)
3 Roles needing a college diploma under 2 years, apprenticeship under 2 years, or 6+ months on-the-job training, bakers, dental assistants, retail sales supervisors Skill Level B (part)
4 Roles needing a high school diploma or several weeks of training, long-haul truck drivers, home support workers, food service supervisors Skill Level C
5 Roles needing short demonstration and no formal education, food counter attendants, cashiers, harvesters Skill Level D

If you remember nothing else from this article, remember that the first digit is your TEER, and your TEER determines almost everything that follows.

Why the NOC code matters so much

Three of the biggest decisions in your file are downstream of this code:

  1. Program eligibility. Express Entry's Federal Skilled Worker Program requires TEER 0, 1, 2 or 3 work experience. The Canadian Experience Class is the same. Most Provincial Nominee Program (PNP) streams either gate on TEER level or list specific NOC codes that qualify. If you pick a TEER 4 code thinking it sounds close enough, you may be screened out before an officer reads a single duty.
  2. CRS points for work experience. Your CRS score for foreign and Canadian work experience is calculated against the NOC code you claim. Officers verify that experience against the NOC 2021 main duties, not against your job title or your salary.
  3. Category-based draws. IRCC has been issuing targeted Express Entry invitations for specific NOC codes in healthcare, STEM, trades, transport, and agriculture. The wrong code can mean missing every draw you were otherwise competitive for. For more on this, see our guide on TEER 0, 1, 2 and 3 jobs that qualify for Express Entry.

NOC mismatch is a top cause of refusal

When an IRCC officer opens your file, they do not search for your job title on Indeed. They open the NOC 2021 entry for the code you claimed and read the lead statement and main duties section. Then they compare those duties, line by line, against your reference letter from your employer.

If your reference letter says you "managed the cash register, restocked shelves, and assisted customers" and you claimed NOC 60020 (retail and wholesale trade managers, TEER 0), the officer will see immediately that your duties describe a retail salesperson (TEER 4), not a manager. The application gets refused for misrepresentation of work experience, or in milder cases, your CRS score is recalculated downward and you lose your invitation.

The rule of thumb our office uses with clients: at least 50% of your day-to-day responsibilities, plus the lead statement, plus most of the main duties, must align with the NOC entry you pick. If you cannot honestly meet that threshold, you have the wrong code.

Where to find your NOC code

There is exactly one authoritative source: noc.esdc.gc.ca, the official NOC 2021 site run by ESDC. Anything else. Reddit threads, immigration forums, AI chatbots, even old blog posts that still show four-digit codes, is secondary and frequently out of date.

On the official site you can:

  • Search by job title in the search bar.
  • Browse by TEER if you already know roughly where you sit.
  • Browse by broad occupational category (digits 1–9 after the TEER digit).

IRCC also publishes a NOC 2016 to NOC 2021 transition table that maps every old four-digit code to its new five-digit code (or codes, some occupations were split or merged in the reclassification).

Step-by-step: picking the right code

Here is the exact sequence we run through with clients in our Abbotsford office:

Step 1. Search by job title, but treat results as candidates, not answers. Type your title. You will usually get 3 to 8 plausible matches. Open all of them in separate tabs.

Step 2. Read the lead statement on each entry. The lead statement is the first paragraph after the code and title. It is the single most important line in the entire NOC entry. It defines who performs this work, where, and at what level of authority. If the lead statement does not describe you, the rest does not matter.

Step 3. Read every main duty. Do not skim. Print the page if you have to. For each duty, ask yourself: "Did I do this regularly, weekly or more, in my actual job?"

Step 4. Apply the 50% test. Count the duties that genuinely match your work. If at least half of them describe your real responsibilities, and the lead statement fits, that is your code. If fewer than half match, keep looking.

Step 5. Cross-check the example titles. Each NOC entry lists example job titles included in that classification and titles explicitly excluded. The exclusion list is where most people discover they have been looking at the wrong code for weeks.

Step 6. Get a reference letter that mirrors the language. Once you have picked the code, ask your employer for a reference letter that lists your actual duties using language that aligns with the NOC main duties. Not copied verbatim, that triggers misrepresentation flags, but using equivalent terminology.

Common edge cases that trip people up

The "sales manager" problem. Sales manager, sales supervisor, account manager, business development manager, and key account executive can all map to completely different NOC codes (TEER 0 management, TEER 1 specialist, TEER 3 supervisor, or TEER 4 sales rep). Title is noise. Duties are signal.

Hybrid roles. If you genuinely split your time between two jobs (say, 60% bookkeeping and 40% office administration), pick the NOC code that reflects the dominant duties. Document the split in your cover letter so the officer is not surprised when your reference letter mentions both.

Roles that have changed over time. If you started as a junior and were promoted, you may have legitimately worked in two NOC codes over your tenure. Claim them separately, with dated reference letters for each phase.

Your job exists in Canada under a different name. Some occupations have local naming conventions. A "controller" in Brazil might be an "accounting manager" in NOC terms. Read the duties, not the brand of the title.

NOC 2016 to NOC 2021: what changed

Some occupations were reclassified between the two systems. A few well-known shifts:

  • The old four-tier Skill Level system (0, A, B, C, D) was replaced by the six-level TEER system.
  • Many roles that were Skill Level B were split between TEER 2 (longer training) and TEER 3 (shorter training).
  • Sixteen occupations that were not eligible under the old Skill Level C now qualify under TEER 3, including transport truck drivers, heavy equipment operators, aircraft assemblers, and several agricultural occupations. This change opened Express Entry eligibility to thousands of workers.

If you have older immigration documents with a four-digit NOC code, do not reuse it. Look up the new five-digit equivalent on the ESDC transition table before you submit anything.

When no code fits perfectly

It happens. Some real-world jobs do not map cleanly onto any single NOC 2021 entry. In that situation:

  1. Pick the closest match by duties (not by title).
  2. Write a short, calm explanation in your cover letter or letter of explanation, stating which code you chose, which duties matched, which did not, and why this is still the most accurate classification available.
  3. Provide a detailed reference letter that lets the officer see the same logic you saw.

Officers respect transparency. They distrust applicants who pick an aspirational code and hope nobody notices.

Common pitfalls

  • Assuming the title matters. It does not. Duties matter.
  • Submitting without a duties-based reference letter. A letter that only confirms dates and salary will not save a contested NOC code.
  • Claiming a higher TEER than your duties support. A TEER 3 supervisor claiming TEER 0 management is a classic refusal pattern.
  • Using outdated four-digit codes. NOC 2016 codes are no longer accepted for new applications.
  • Trusting a single AI or forum answer. Verify on noc.esdc.gc.ca every time.

Appendix. Fact-check notes

  • NOC 2021 is the current system. Adopted by IRCC for all economic immigration programs on November 16, 2022. Replaced NOC 2016.
  • Format is 5 digits. First digit is the TEER level (0–5). The remaining four digits identify the specific occupation within the broad category.
