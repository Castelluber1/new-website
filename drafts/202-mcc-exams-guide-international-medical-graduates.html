MCC Exams for International Medical Graduates: MCCQE1, NAC OSCE, and MCCQE2 Explained

10 min read•2026-06-12

If you are a foreign-trained physician planning to practice in Canada, the Medical Council of Canada (MCC) exams are the gateway you cannot go around. Every province requires MCC exam results as part of its licensing process. No Canadian residency program will consider your application without them. They are not optional and they are not interchangeable with the licensing exams from other countries.

The good news is that the MCC exam structure is predictable. There are three exams, they are taken in a defined sequence, and each one tests a specific set of competencies. Understanding what each exam involves, in what order to take them, and how to prepare effectively will save you time and prevent the expensive mistake of sitting an exam before you are ready.

This guide breaks down all three MCC exams for international medical graduates (IMGs): what each one covers, how it is structured, what to expect on exam day, and how to sequence your preparation.

The Three MCC Exams: Quick Comparison

Exam Format Length What It Tests When to Take It
MCCQE Part 1 Computer-based (MCQ + CDM) 1 day Clinical knowledge across all disciplines First. Can be taken outside Canada.
NAC OSCE In-person, standardized patient stations ~2 hours (12 stations x 10 min) Clinical skills, communication, patient management After Part 1. Requires travel to Canada.
MCCQE Part 2 In-person OSCE Multiple stations Clinical decision-making in realistic scenarios Usually during or after residency. Required for full licensure in most provinces.

All three exams require you to hold a valid MCC Source of Standing before registering. If you have not yet applied for credential verification with the MCC, that step comes first. For a full overview of the pathway from credential verification through residency and provincial licensing, see our guide: How to Practice Medicine in Canada as a Foreign-Trained Physician.

MCCQE Part 1: Clinical Knowledge Foundation

What it is

The Medical Council of Canada Qualifying Examination Part 1, known as MCCQE Part 1 or simply Part 1, is a computer-based exam that tests your clinical knowledge and judgment. It is the first licensing exam most IMGs sit, and it is the prerequisite for the NAC OSCE in most pathways.

Format and structure

Part 1 consists of 210 questions in two item types:

  • Single Best Answer (SBA): Standard multiple-choice questions with four or five options. You select the single most correct answer. These questions test knowledge of clinical presentations, diagnostic reasoning, treatment options, and clinical management.
  • Clinical Decision-Making (CDM): Extended clinical vignettes that require you to develop a diagnosis, order investigations, or outline a management plan. These are not multiple choice. You type or select responses in a structured format that simulates clinical documentation.

The SBA section is taken in the morning. The CDM section is taken in the afternoon. Total exam time is a full day.

Where and when it is offered

Part 1 is computer-based and administered through Prometric testing centers. Prometric has locations in dozens of countries, including Brazil, the United States, and across Europe. This means you can sit Part 1 while still living in your home country, without requiring Canadian immigration status. That is a significant advantage: most IMGs who build a strong Canada plan sit Part 1 abroad and begin their immigration process while waiting for or receiving their results.

The exam is offered year-round, though seats at popular Prometric centers book up. Check medicalcouncil.ca for current registration windows and available dates at centers near you.

What it covers

Part 1 covers the full breadth of medical practice: internal medicine, surgery, pediatrics, obstetrics and gynecology, psychiatry, dermatology, ophthalmology, emergency medicine, and preventive care. Questions are framed in a Canadian clinical context, which means they reference Canadian clinical guidelines, health system structures, and standard-of-care benchmarks that may differ from your home country.

This context-shift is one of the underestimated preparation challenges for IMGs. You may know the diagnosis and the treatment, but the exam asks what you would do in a Canadian emergency department, following Canadian triage protocols, with Canadian drug formularies in mind. Preparation materials that are specific to the MCC, not generic USMLE prep resources, will address this.

Pass rates and what to expect

The MCC publishes aggregate pass rate data on its website. IMG pass rates for Part 1 have historically been lower than Canadian medical graduate rates. Check medicalcouncil.ca for the most current published data, as rates are updated annually. The gap in pass rates does not reflect a difference in physician capability. It reflects a difference in preparation for the Canadian clinical context specifically. IMGs who use Canada-specific prep resources and allow adequate study time close this gap significantly.

NAC OSCE: Clinical Skills Under Observation

What it is

The National Assessment Collaboration Objective Structured Clinical Examination, known as the NAC OSCE, is the most different exam in the MCC sequence from anything most IMGs have previously experienced. It does not test what you know. It tests what you do in a clinical encounter.

Format and structure

The NAC OSCE consists of 12 stations. At each station you have 10 minutes with a standardized patient (or a written scenario for non-patient stations). You are assessed by a trained examiner who watches your encounter and scores you on a structured checklist.

Across the 12 stations, you are evaluated on:

  • History taking: Your ability to gather a relevant, organized, empathetic history in a time-pressured encounter
  • Physical examination: Whether you perform an appropriate, correctly sequenced physical exam and explain it to the patient as you go
  • Communication: How clearly and respectfully you communicate with the patient, including how you handle sensitive disclosures (bad news, mental health, substance use)
  • Clinical reasoning and management: Whether your differential diagnosis and management plan are appropriate given the history and findings

There is a 2-minute reading period before each station where you review the scenario outside the room. You then enter, perform the encounter, and exit when time is called.

Where and when it is offered

The NAC OSCE is offered twice per year at testing centers in Canada. It requires in-person attendance, which means you must have Canadian immigration status that permits you to travel to Canada for the exam. A visitor visa or electronic travel authorization (eTA) may be sufficient depending on your nationality and the circumstances of your travel, but you should confirm this with an immigration professional before booking.

Because the NAC OSCE is offered only twice per year, failing and retrying adds at minimum six months to your timeline. Many successful IMGs take 12 to 18 months of dedicated preparation before sitting.

What the stations look like

Stations cover a wide range of presentations. Based on the MCC's published objectives, common station types include:

  • A patient presenting with chest pain (history, differential, initial management)
  • A child brought in by a parent with fever and rash (pediatric history, examination, reassurance and discharge planning)
  • A patient with depression or suicidal ideation (mental status, risk assessment, communication)
  • Delivering a new diagnosis or test result to a patient (communication skills under stress)
  • An emergency presentation requiring rapid assessment and stabilization (clinical decision-making under time pressure)

The MCC publishes a detailed list of clinical presentations and objectives on its website. This list is your study guide for the NAC OSCE. Every item on it is fair game.

Why IMGs find this exam particularly challenging

Most IMGs trained in systems where physician-patient communication styles differ from Canadian norms. Canadian clinical culture emphasizes shared decision-making, patient autonomy, informed consent, and a collaborative rather than directive approach. Examiners score communication explicitly, not just clinical knowledge.

Practicing with standardized patients in mock OSCE formats before sitting the real exam is not optional if you want to pass on the first attempt. Courses that simulate the full 12-station format under timed conditions are widely available in major Canadian cities and online.

MCCQE Part 2: Clinical Decision-Making for Licensure

What it is

The MCCQE Part 2 is an OSCE-format examination that tests clinical decision-making in realistic patient scenarios. Unlike the NAC OSCE, which assesses your clinical and communication process, Part 2 focuses on the quality of your clinical decisions: the right workup, the right diagnosis, the right management plan.

Who needs it and when

Most provincial licensing bodies require Part 2 for full, independent practice licensure. Some provinces accept Part 1 and NAC OSCE results for provisional or supervised practice while a physician completes Part 2, but this is province-specific and the rules change. Check with the college in your target province for their current requirements.

The typical sequence is to sit Part 2 during residency, when your clinical decision-making has been refined through supervised practice. Many programs build Part 2 preparation into their residency curriculum.

Format

Part 2 is delivered in an OSCE format with standardized patient stations focused on clinical decision-making scenarios. Candidates are assessed on their ability to gather targeted clinical information, synthesize findings, and arrive at appropriate diagnostic and management decisions. The exam is offered at Canadian testing sites on scheduled dates. Check medicalcouncil.ca for current scheduling.

Preparation Resources and Timeline

Recommended preparation timeline

Exam Recommended Preparation Window
MCCQE Part 1 12 to 18 months of structured study, especially if you trained outside a Canadian or American system
NAC OSCE 6 to 12 months after Part 1, including significant standardized patient practice
MCCQE Part 2 3 to 6 months during residency, depending on your clinical exposure

These are general benchmarks. IMGs who have recently practiced in clinical environments and have strong English medical communication skills may need less preparation time. IMGs who have been out of clinical practice for several years, or who trained in a non-English system, typically need more.

Preparation resources to know

  • MCC Objectives for the Qualifying Examination: The official content blueprint published by the MCC. Everything on the exam is derived from this document. It is freely available on medicalcouncil.ca.
  • Canada QBank and similar Canada-specific question banks: These use Canadian clinical context in their questions, which makes them more relevant than USMLE resources for MCC prep.
  • NAC prep courses: Multiple organizations in Canada offer OSCE prep courses with standardized patient practice. Several now offer virtual formats for candidates still abroad.
  • CARMSNavigator and IMG-specific communities: Peer support from other IMGs who have recently sat the exams is a legitimate prep resource. Exam formats and common scenarios change, and recent candidates are your best source of current experience.

Sequence and Strategy: How to Approach All Three Exams

The standard recommended sequence is Part 1, then NAC OSCE, then Part 2. There are practical and logistical reasons for this:

  • Part 1 is required before you can register for the NAC OSCE in most circumstances
  • The NAC OSCE is required before CaRMS will consider your application competitive
  • Part 2 is typically done during residency, not before

The question many IMGs ask is whether to complete all three exams before applying for residency or immigration. The answer depends on your specific situation. For CaRMS competitiveness, having Part 1 and NAC OSCE results is the standard expectation. Having Part 2 results as well is a strong asset but not always required to match.

For immigration purposes, having exam results strengthens an Express Entry profile. But waiting to apply for permanent residence until after all three exams means waiting 3 to 5 years. A better strategy is to begin immigration planning as soon as you have Part 1 results and a clear exam trajectory.

Exam Fees: What to Budget

Exam fees change annually and vary based on currency exchange rates for international candidates. Rather than publishing specific dollar amounts that will quickly become outdated, we recommend checking medicalcouncil.ca directly for current fee schedules.

As a general planning note: between registration fees, preparation materials, potential retake costs, and travel for in-person exams, IMGs commonly budget several thousand Canadian dollars for the full MCC exam sequence. Factor this into your Canada plan alongside immigration and living costs.

The Immigration Layer

Sitting MCC exams and pursuing immigration to Canada are not separate planning tracks. They affect each other at multiple points:

  • The NAC OSCE and MCCQE Part 2 require in-person attendance in Canada. You need valid status to enter.
  • CaRMS residency applications benefit from Canadian clinical experience. Gaining that experience requires work or study authorization.
  • Residency itself requires a work permit or permanent resident status.
  • Your CRS score for Express Entry may be strengthened by Canadian exam credentials and provincial nominations for healthcare workers.

If you are currently outside Canada and beginning this process, the immigration question is not one you can defer. Getting your immigration status structured correctly from the start will determine whether your exam timeline translates into a Canadian career or a long delay while you sort out the status issues you skipped.
