PGWP: Everything You Need to Know About the Post-Graduation Work Permit in 2026

10 min read•2026-05-16

The Post-Graduation Work Permit (PGWP) used to be the simplest, most predictable step in the international student-to-PR pipeline. Finish an eligible program at a designated learning institution, apply within 180 days, and walk away with up to three years of open work authorization in Canada. That predictability is gone. Between October 2024 and early 2025, IRCC rewrote almost every rule that mattered: the field-of-study eligibility list, spousal open work permit access, and the documentation IRCC will actually accept. If you started your program in 2023 thinking you understood the PGWP, you no longer do. This guide explains the post-graduation work permit as it actually works in 2026, what changed, who still qualifies, and how to avoid the mistakes that are now causing thousands of refusals.

What the PGWP Actually Is

The Post-Graduation Work Permit is an open work permit. That means it is not tied to a specific employer, occupation, job offer, or province. You can work full-time, part-time, switch jobs, work for multiple employers, be self-employed, or sit unemployed while you job-hunt. There is no labour market impact assessment (LMIA) requirement, no employer sponsorship, no provincial nomination tied to it. The PGWP exists because Canada wants international graduates to gain Canadian work experience, because Canadian work experience is the single most valuable input into a successful Express Entry profile through the Canadian Experience Class (CEC).

The PGWP is also a once-in-a-lifetime permit. You get one PGWP, ever. If you study, get a PGWP, leave Canada, return as a student, and graduate again, you do not get a second PGWP. This rule has not changed, but it has become more consequential as program eligibility tightens, because graduates who chose the wrong program now have no second chance.

Eligibility in 2026: The Four Hard Gates

To qualify for a PGWP in 2026, you must pass four gates. Missing any one of them ends the application.

Gate 1: Designated Learning Institution (DLI) on the PGWP-eligible list. Not every DLI qualifies for the PGWP. Public colleges and universities almost always do. Private career colleges almost never do. The list is published and updated by IRCC, and a school's status can change between the time you apply and the time you graduate. Always verify against the current PGWP-eligible DLI list before paying tuition.

Gate 2: Field-of-study eligibility (the January 2025 change). This is the rule that has caught the most people off guard. As of program start dates on or after November 1, 2024, IRCC requires that college-level programs (non-degree programs) lead to an occupation on its list of long-term in-demand fields. Bachelor's, master's, and doctoral degrees from public universities are exempt from the field-of-study list, but college diplomas and certificates are not. If your college program does not map to an eligible field, no PGWP, regardless of grades, completion, or DLI status.

Gate 3: Program length of at least 8 months. Your program of study must have been at least eight months long, full-time, leading to a degree, diploma, or certificate. Programs shorter than eight months do not qualify. Part-time studies do not qualify, with one narrow exception for the final academic session.

Gate 4: In-person completion. The COVID-era flexibility that allowed online study to count toward PGWP eligibility ended on September 1, 2024. Students who started programs after that date must complete their studies in person in Canada. Distance learning and online programs no longer lead to a PGWP. There are transitional rules for students who started during the COVID flexibility window, but anyone starting fresh in 2026 needs to be physically studying in Canada.

The Field-of-Study List: What's In and What's Out

The field-of-study list is the single biggest source of confusion in 2026. IRCC built it around long-term labour shortages identified by Employment and Social Development Canada. The list maps programs to Classification of Instructional Programs (CIP) codes, which then map to in-demand occupations. The list is updated periodically, so always verify your specific CIP code before enrolling.

Critically, university degree programs (bachelor's, master's, PhD) from public universities are not subject to this filter. A history major at the University of Toronto still qualifies for a PGWP. A history-equivalent program at a public college does not.

How Long Will Your PGWP Be?

PGWP duration is tied directly to program length, with a hard cap.

  • Programs at least 8 months but less than 2 years: PGWP length matches program length. A 1-year program gets you a 1-year PGWP. A 16-month program gets you a 16-month PGWP.
  • Programs of 2 years or longer: 3-year PGWP, the maximum.
  • Master's degree programs of at least 8 months: 3-year PGWP, regardless of program length. This is the master's exception, and it is one of the most valuable rules in the system. A 10-month master's program in a public university still gets you a 3-year permit.

Passport validity can cut your PGWP short. IRCC issues the permit only up to the expiry date of your passport. If your passport expires in 18 months and you qualify for a 3-year PGWP, you will receive an 18-month permit and need to apply to extend later. Renew your passport before applying.

The 180-Day Window: Apply Fast or Lose It

You have 180 calendar days from the date you receive written confirmation that you have completed your program to submit a complete PGWP application. The clock starts when your institution issues the official completion letter or final transcript, not when you finish your last exam, not when convocation happens, not when your degree is conferred. Get the completion letter in writing, note the date, and apply quickly.

During this 180-day window, you can be inside Canada or outside. You can apply from Canada if your study permit is still valid, or on a visitor record, or even after your study permit has expired (with implied status rules). You can apply from outside Canada too, though that route is less common. What you cannot do is wait past day 180 and try to negotiate. The deadline is hard.

If your study permit expires during the 180 days and you have submitted a PGWP application from inside Canada, you have implied status to work full-time while waiting for a decision, provided your study permit was valid when you applied. This is a major change from older rules and is worth confirming with a regulated immigration consultant before relying on it.

Required Documents

A complete PGWP application typically includes:

  • Proof of program completion: An official letter from your institution confirming you met all program requirements, with the completion date.
  • Official transcript: Final transcript showing all courses completed and the credential awarded.
  • Letter of program of study (PoS): Document from your DLI confirming program length, format (in-person vs. online), and CIP code where relevant.
  • Valid passport: Must extend past the requested PGWP duration if you want the full term.
  • Biometrics: Required unless previously given within the last 10 years.
  • Application fees: Processing fee plus open work permit holder fee.
  • Digital photo meeting IRCC specifications.

Missing documents are the most common avoidable cause of refusal. IRCC does not chase you for missing items as forgivingly as it used to. Submit a complete package the first time.

PGWP as the Bridge to Permanent Residence

The PGWP exists because IRCC wants a pipeline from study to permanent residence. The mechanism is the Canadian Experience Class (CEC) stream of Express Entry. To qualify for CEC, you need at least one year (1,560 hours) of full-time skilled work experience in Canada within the last three years, in an occupation classified at TEER 0, 1, 2, or 3 under the National Occupational Classification.

The strategy is straightforward in theory: graduate, get the PGWP, find a skilled job, log 12 months, file an Express Entry profile, and target a CEC draw. In practice, CRS cutoffs and category-based draws now make CEC less of a guaranteed path than it once was. French language proficiency, work experience in priority sectors (healthcare, trades, STEM), and provincial nominations all play larger roles than they did three years ago. The PGWP is the runway, but the takeoff still requires planning. See our breakdown of the Canadian Experience Class for current requirements.

Spouse Open Work Permit: The Rule You Probably Missed

Until 2024, the spouse of any PGWP holder could apply for a spousal open work permit (SOWP). That rule no longer exists. As of January 2025, spousal OWP eligibility for PGWP holders is restricted to spouses of graduates in:

  • Master's degree programs of at least 16 months
  • Doctoral (PhD) programs
  • Specific professional programs (medicine, dentistry, pharmacy, law, optometry, veterinary medicine, education, and certain engineering disciplines)

If you have a PGWP from a bachelor's program or a 2-year college diploma, your spouse no longer qualifies for an open work permit through your status. They can still come on a visitor visa, study permit, or their own work permit, but the automatic spousal OWP is gone for most PGWP holders. This is one of the most consequential changes in the entire international student system. See our deeper coverage of the spouse open work permit changes.

Common Mistakes That Cause PGWP Refusals

After 18 months of working through the new rules with clients, these are the failure modes we see most often.

  1. Choosing a DLI that is not on the PGWP-eligible list. This is almost always preventable and almost always fatal. Verify the school's PGWP status before paying tuition, not after graduation.
  2. Choosing a college program in a field that is not on the eligibility list. A culinary diploma, a fashion certificate, a sports management program from a public college — all leave the graduate with no PGWP, no Canadian work experience runway, and no second chance.
  3. Missing the 180-day deadline. Often because the student waits for "everything to be perfect" instead of submitting on time. The application can be supplemented or corrected; a missed deadline cannot.
  4. Studying partially online after September 2024. Students assume the COVID flexibility is still in effect. It is not. In-person attendance now matters.
  5. Letting the passport expire too soon. Renew the passport before applying so IRCC issues the full PGWP duration.
  6. Assuming spouse OWP eligibility. Couples plan their move based on outdated information and discover the spouse cannot work three months in.
  7. Applying with an incomplete package. Missing transcripts, missing completion letters, missing biometrics. IRCC refuses rather than chases.

The Bigger Picture

The PGWP is now a much more selective program than it was three years ago. Canada is no longer issuing a blanket invitation to anyone with a college diploma. The program has been recalibrated around occupations Canada actually needs filled: healthcare, trades, agriculture, transport, STEM. If you are in one of those fields, your path is clearer than ever and the work permit will still anchor a strong PR strategy. If you are not, you need to know that before you spend $40,000 on tuition, not after.

The right move before enrolling in any Canadian program is to verify three things, in this order: is the DLI on the PGWP-eligible list, does the program lead to a field of study on the eligibility list, and does the program length plus expected PGWP duration give you a realistic runway to one year of skilled work plus a competitive Express Entry profile. If any of those answers is uncertain, get advice before you commit.

Appendix: Fact-Check Notes

  • Field-of-study eligibility list: IRCC announced in September 2024 that the field-of-study requirement would apply to college-level programs (non-degree) for international students whose study permit applications were received on or after November 1, 2024. University degree programs (bachelor's, master's, doctoral) at public universities remain exempt from the field-of-study list. The list is built around occupations in long-term shortage as identified by ESDC and published with CIP code mappings. Verify the current list and CIP codes at IRCC's official PGWP page before publication.
  • 180-day window: Confirmed. PGWP applications must be submitted within 180 calendar days of written confirmation of program completion. This rule predates 2024 and has not been altered by the recent changes.
  • 8-month minimum: Confirmed and unchanged. Program must be at least 8 months in length, full-time, leading to a degree, diploma, or certificate.
  • 3-year cap: Confirmed. Maximum PGWP duration is 3 years. Programs of 2 years or longer qualify for the full 3 years. Master's programs of at least 8 months qualify for 3 years regardless of program length.
  • Spouse OWP changes: IRCC announced in January 2025 that spousal OWP eligibility for PGWP holders would be restricted to spouses of graduates in master's degree programs (16+ months), doctoral programs, and specific professional programs. Verify the current list of qualifying professional programs before publication, as the list has been adjusted since announcement.
  • Online study post-September 2024: The COVID-era online study flexibility for PGWP eligibility ended September 1, 2024. Students whose programs started after that date must complete studies in person in Canada. Transitional rules apply for students who started during the flexibility window.
  • One PGWP per lifetime: Confirmed and unchanged.
  • Passport-limited duration: Confirmed. IRCC will not issue a PGWP that extends beyond the passport expiry date.

Recommended verification sources at time of publication: IRCC's official PGWP page, the PGWP-eligible DLI list, the current field-of-study eligibility list with CIP codes, and the January 2025 ministerial announcement on spousal OWP changes.
