Study Permits

Canada Study Permit Checklist 2026: Every Document You Need

10 min read•2026-05-16

The Canada study permit process changed more in the last 18 months than in the previous decade. The Student Direct Stream (SDS) — the fast-track program most international students relied on — was killed on November 8, 2024. Provincial attestation letters became mandatory for almost every undergraduate application in January 2024. A national study permit cap is now in its second year, and field-of-study restrictions decide who keeps a post-graduation work permit after graduation.

If you are applying in 2026, the checklist you found on a Reddit thread from 2023 will get you refused. Here is the current document list, the channel to file through, and the order to gather everything in so your application is approved on the first try.

What changed: SDS is gone, regular stream is the only path

Until November 2024, students from 14 countries (including Brazil, India, China, Mexico, Vietnam, and the Philippines) could apply through SDS and get a decision in roughly 20 calendar days. The trade-off: you needed a Guaranteed Investment Certificate (GIC) of CAD $20,635, full first-year tuition paid up front, and a clean medical exam.

IRCC ended SDS on November 8, 2024. Every applicant — regardless of nationality — now files through the regular study permit stream. The regular stream is slower, more documentation-heavy, and gives the visa officer more room to refuse for "weak ties to home country" or "unclear study plan." That is why this checklist matters more than it did two years ago.

The GIC still exists and is still useful (more on that below), but it no longer guarantees faster processing or a special review track.

The 2026 study permit document checklist

Below is the complete list of documents IRCC expects in a regular-stream application. Missing one of the first six is an almost-automatic refusal.

Document Required? Notes
Letter of Acceptance (LOA) from a DLI Yes Must be a Designated Learning Institution. For PGWP eligibility, the program must also be on the field-of-study list.
Provincial Attestation Letter (PAL) or Territorial Attestation Letter (TAL) Yes (most cases) Mandatory for almost all undergraduate, college, and language program applications since January 22, 2024. Master's, PhD, and select exempt programs do not need one.
Proof of funds Yes CAD $22,895 for a single applicant for 12 months of living costs, plus first-year tuition, plus travel costs. Updated by IRCC for 2024 and still in effect for 2026 applications.
Valid passport Yes Must cover the full intended stay. Renew before you apply if it expires in less than two years.
Biometrics Yes CAD $85 fee, collected at a VAC after you submit. Valid for 10 years.

Immigration Medical Exam (IME) Conditional Required if you have lived 6+ months in a designated country in the past year, or if you will study/work in healthcare, childcare, or primary/secondary education.

The DLI list: two lists you need to check, not one

A Designated Learning Institution is a school approved by a province or territory to host international students. The full DLI list lives on the IRCC website and is updated regularly. If your school is not on it, your application will be refused — no exceptions.

There is a second list that most applicants miss. In January 2025, IRCC published a field-of-study list for post-graduation work permit (PGWP) eligibility. If you are planning to work in Canada after graduation, your program of study must appear on that list at the time you apply for the study permit. Roughly 178 fields qualify, concentrated in healthcare, STEM, trades, transportation, and agriculture. Programs outside the list (most business diplomas, hospitality, general arts) no longer lead to a PGWP for college-level students. University degree holders are largely exempt from the field-of-study restriction but still need to verify program eligibility before enrolling.

Check both lists before you pay any tuition deposit. A DLI-approved program that does not appear on the field-of-study list is a strategic dead end for anyone whose plan includes working in Canada after school.

Provincial Attestation Letter (PAL): the one that breaks most applications

Since January 22, 2024, almost every undergraduate, college, and language program applicant needs a PAL (or TAL in the territories) from the province where they intend to study. The PAL is the province's confirmation that you are counted within their share of the national study permit cap.

How to get one: you do not request a PAL directly. The school requests it from the province on your behalf after you accept the offer of admission and pay any required deposit. Processing time varies wildly — some Ontario colleges turn it around in 48 hours, others take six to eight weeks. Quebec uses a different document (the CAQ + PAL combination).

Programs exempt from the PAL requirement: master's and doctoral degrees, in-Canada study permit extensions, primary and secondary school, exchange students at DLIs with formal agreements, and a small list of edge cases. If you are applying for a bachelor's degree, college diploma, or language program, assume you need a PAL until your school confirms otherwise.

Submitting an application without a PAL when one is required = refusal. Do not file until the PAL is in your hands.

Proof of funds: what counts and what doesn't

IRCC raised the financial requirement in 2024 to CAD $22,895 for a single applicant covering 12 months of living costs. That figure is in addition to tuition and travel costs. Add roughly CAD $4,000 per accompanying family member.

Acceptable evidence (any combination that reaches the threshold):

  • Bank statements: the last 4 months of activity from your personal account, showing a consistent balance, not a single large deposit a week before filing. Officers flag suspicious "money parked" patterns.
  • GIC: a Guaranteed Investment Certificate from a participating Canadian bank (Scotiabank, RBC, CIBC, ICICI Bank Canada, others). Even with SDS gone, a GIC is still one of the cleanest ways to prove funds because it is dollar-locked in Canada in your name. Many officers still treat a full GIC favorably.
  • Scholarship or assistantship letter: must be on institutional letterhead, name you specifically, and state the amount and disbursement schedule.
  • Education loan: from a recognized bank, with a sanction letter showing the amount, your name, and disbursement terms.
  • Sponsor's bank statements + affidavit: if a parent or relative is funding you, include their last 4 months of statements, an affidavit of financial support, proof of relationship, and ideally their employment letter and income tax returns.

What does not count: gifted property, crypto holdings, business inventory, or "promise to pay" letters without an underlying bank balance.

The study plan: why it matters more after SDS

Under SDS, study plans were often skimmed. Under the regular stream, they are read. A study plan (sometimes called a statement of purpose or letter of explanation) is your chance to answer the questions the officer is silently asking: Why this program? Why this school? Why Canada? Why not at home? What will you do after graduation?

A weak study plan is now the number-one refusal driver we see at UP Immigration. Officers refuse for "purpose of visit not established" when the applicant cannot connect the program to a credible career outcome in their home country.

We wrote a full breakdown on how to write one that survives review: Study plan que convence: como escrever o seu. The short version is that one page of generic motivation will get you refused. Two to three pages tying the program to specific outcomes will not.

How to apply: online only, through your IRCC account

Paper applications for study permits have effectively been phased out for most countries. You apply online by creating an IRCC secure account, completing the IMM 1294 form digitally, uploading every supporting document as a PDF, paying the CAD $150 application fee plus CAD $85 biometrics fee, and submitting.

After submission you receive a Biometrics Instruction Letter within 24 to 48 hours. You then book an appointment at a Visa Application Centre in your country to give fingerprints and a photo. Biometrics are valid for 10 years, so if you gave them for a prior Canadian application you may be exempt.

Processing times and the 2026 cap

IRCC publishes processing times weekly. As of early 2026, regular-stream study permit applications from outside Canada are averaging 8 to 14 weeks depending on the visa office. Brazil is currently around 9 weeks. India and the Philippines are longer.

The national study permit cap remains in place for 2026 at approximately 437,000 new permits — a roughly 10% reduction from the 2025 ceiling. Provincial allocations are tighter. Apply early, especially for September intakes, because PAL issuance slows as provinces approach their cap.

Common refusal reasons in 2026

The top five reasons IRCC refuses study permits right now:

  1. Purpose of visit not established — weak study plan, no clear link between program and career.
  2. Insufficient funds — failed to hit CAD $22,895 + tuition + travel, or funds look freshly deposited.
  3. Travel history / ties to home country — officer doubts you will leave Canada after studies.
  4. Misrepresentation — discrepancies between forms, transcripts, employment letters. Triggers a 5-year ban.
  5. Program not a reasonable progression — applying for a diploma after already holding a master's, with no logical explanation.

If you have already been refused, do not just re-submit the same package. Read Study permit refused in Canada: reasons and next steps before filing again. A GCMS notes request is almost always the right first move.

After approval: port of entry

Approval does not equal a study permit. What IRCC sends you is a Port of Entry (POE) Letter of Introduction. The actual study permit is printed by a CBSA officer when you land in Canada.

Bring to the airport: the POE letter, your passport with the visa or eTA, your Letter of Acceptance, your PAL, proof of funds, and any custodian declaration if applicable. Be ready to answer simple questions about your program, your school, and how you will support yourself. Most interviews last under three minutes. Inconsistent answers between your file and what you say at the booth can result in a permit being denied at the border — even after IRCC approval.

If you receive a study permit with conditions (work hours, off-campus restrictions, change of program prohibited), read the conditions carefully on the spot and ask the officer to correct any errors before you leave the secondary inspection area.
