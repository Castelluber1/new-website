IRCC Processing Times 2026: How Long Every Canadian Immigration Application Actually Takes

10 min read•2026-05-17

The single most asked question in our Abbotsford office, on every consultation call, in every WhatsApp message from clients in São Paulo or Belo Horizonte, is the same: "How long will my application take?" It is the question that dictates when families book their flights, when international students accept university offers, when employers count on a foreign worker showing up at the warehouse. And it is the question with the most confusing public answer, because Immigration, Refugees and Citizenship Canada (IRCC) publishes processing times in a way that most applicants misread.

This guide gives you the complete, current 2026 picture for every major IRCC application type, explains exactly how those numbers are calculated, flags the Brazil-specific quirks that affect our clients most, and tells you what to do when your file goes silent.

How IRCC actually measures processing time

Before you look at any number, you need to understand the methodology, because misreading it is the source of 90% of the panic emails RCICs receive.

IRCC publishes what it calls the "service standard" or "target processing time," and that number represents the time it took to finalize 80% of applications in the same category over the most recent backwards-looking window (usually 8 to 16 weeks of historical data, depending on the program). It is not an average, not a median, and definitely not a promise.

What that means in plain English:

  • If the published time for a study permit is 8 weeks, 20% of applicants will wait longer, sometimes much longer.
  • The clock starts when IRCC receives a complete application, with biometrics submitted, fees paid, and all forms uploaded correctly. A missing police certificate or a blurry passport scan resets your position effectively to zero.
  • Times are updated weekly on the IRCC website and shift constantly with inventory, staffing, and policy changes.

Keep that 80% rule in mind as you read the table below. If you are inside the published window, you are normal. If you are past it by more than 30%, it is time to start asking questions.

Why your file might take longer than the published time

Being in the slow 20% is not random. There are predictable reasons, and most of them are preventable.

Incomplete documentation. IRCC officers do not call you to ask for a missing translation or a clearer scan. They either return the application (in some cases) or quietly extend processing while sending a procedural fairness letter weeks later. The single highest-leverage thing you can do for your timeline is submit a file that needs zero follow-up.

Security and background screening. Applicants who have lived in countries on enhanced screening lists, who have worked in sensitive industries (defense, nuclear, certain government roles), or who have family ties IRCC wants to verify will see additional security partner reviews. These are invisible from the outside and can add 3 to 18 months to any PR application. Brazilian applicants are rarely flagged for country-of-residence reasons, but prior residence in certain jurisdictions can trigger reviews.

Medical exam complications. Upfront medicals are valid for 12 months. If yours expires before a decision is made, IRCC will request a new one, which often adds 4 to 8 weeks. For files expected to push past 11 months, do not do the medical too early.

Biometrics not received. You have 30 days from the biometrics instruction letter (BIL) to attend a VAC. If the VAC in São Paulo, Rio, Brasília, Recife, or Porto Alegre is booked out, attend the next nearest one. Files sit in "biometrics not received" status indefinitely.

Document verification with third parties. ECA reports from WES, IQAS, or ICES; police certificates from countries with slow systems; employer reference letters where IRCC contacts the employer directly. Any external verification adds time you cannot control.

Policy changes mid-stream. Category-based draws, PNP allocation cuts, study permit caps, all of these create temporary processing surges that distort published times for 8 to 12 weeks after the policy lands. The 2024 study permit cap, for example, doubled processing for some source countries through mid-2025.

How to check your IRCC application status

You have three official tools, and you should use all of them depending on application type.

1. IRCC Secure Account (most applications). Log in at canada.ca, click your application, and read the messages section. Real status updates appear here first.

2. Application Status Tracker (most PR streams, citizenship, sponsorship). A separate, more detailed tool linked from inside your account. Shows the granular steps: eligibility, medicals, security, background, final decision. Worth checking weekly.

3. Client Application Status (legacy CIC tool). Still works for some older application types and for tracking PR card status by UCI.

What you should not do: call the IRCC contact centre to ask "where is my application." Agents have no more visibility than the tracker shows, and the calls do not move your file. Use the contact centre only to update an address, report a change of representative, or request status when you are 30+ days past the published service standard.

GCMS notes, what they are and when to order them

If your application is well past the published processing time and the tracker shows nothing useful, the next step is a GCMS notes request (also called ATIP notes. Access to Information and Privacy).

GCMS (Global Case Management System) is the IRCC database where every officer note, every keystroke, every internal flag on your file is recorded. You are legally entitled to request a copy if you are inside Canada or a Canadian citizen/PR. If you are outside Canada, a Canadian resident representative orders them on your behalf.

What GCMS notes tell you:

  • Exactly what stage your application is at internally (which is often months ahead of what the public tracker shows)
  • Officer concerns or red flags noted on your file
  • Whether security screening is pending, complete, or has triggered enhanced review
  • Specific reasons for refusal if your application was already refused
  • Any procedural fairness letter that was drafted but not yet sent

Cost is $5, and standard turnaround is 30 days. We order GCMS notes for clients automatically when an application crosses 130% of the published service standard. If your file is silent and you are well past target, do not guess, read the notes. The Brazil factor, what changes for Brazilian applicants

Most published IRCC processing times are global averages, but country of residence and country of citizenship change the picture in several specific ways.

Document collection takes longer. Brazilian police certificates, certidões de nascimento and casamento, sworn translation by tradutor juramentado, none of it is fast, and IRCC expects all of it. Plan 6 to 10 weeks of pre-application work for any PR file.

Biometrics centres are spread thin. Brazil has VACs in São Paulo, Rio de Janeiro, Brasília, Recife, and Porto Alegre. Applicants in cities like Manaus, Fortaleza, or Florianópolis face a domestic flight just to give biometrics. Build that into your timeline.

TRV processing is reasonable but not guaranteed. Brazil sits in a moderate-volume bucket for visitor visa applications. A clean, well-documented TRV from a Brazilian applicant typically lands inside 30 days, but files with weak ties to Brazil, prior refusals, or thin financial documentation easily push past 60.

Study permit SDS is your speed advantage. The Student Direct Stream is available to Brazilian nationals and, when applicable, can compress processing to under 6 weeks. Requirements include a CAD $20,635 GIC, first-year tuition paid, valid language test, and a letter of acceptance from a DLI. If you qualify, use it. When to escalate, and how

Most files do not need escalation. The published time is the published time, and IRCC genuinely does its best to hit the 80% standard. But there are three legitimate escalation moments.

1. Web form inquiry (free). Once you are 30 days past the published service standard, you can submit a web form requesting a status update. Responses are slow (4–8 weeks) and often unhelpful, but they create a paper trail.

2. Member of Parliament inquiry. Your local MP's constituency office can file a case-specific inquiry with IRCC and frequently gets a response within 2 to 6 weeks. This is not just for citizens, anyone living in a riding can ask their MP for help. We see this work especially well for spousal and PGP files that are clearly stalled.

3. Federal Court mandamus. When IRCC has unreasonably delayed a decision (typically 24+ months past service standard for PR files, longer for citizenship), a writ of mandamus is the legal tool that forces a decision. It is expensive, it requires an immigration lawyer, and IRCC often issues a decision the moment the application is filed. Reserve this for cases that are genuinely stuck for years.

The bottom line

Published IRCC processing times are a useful planning tool, not a promise. They tell you what 80% of applicants in your category experienced over the past few months, and that number changes weekly. Build your life around the published time plus a 30% buffer, submit a complete file the first time, track your status through the official channels, and escalate through web form → MP → GCMS notes in that order if your file goes silent.
