Enrolling Children in Canadian Schools: A Newcomer Family's 2026 Guide

10 min read•2026-05-16

Getting your kids into a Canadian classroom is usually one of the first real settlement tasks after you land. The good news: Canada makes it relatively straightforward, and most newcomer families have their children in school within a week or two of arrival. The trickier news: the rules are set by each province, not by the federal government, so the school board in Toronto works differently from the one in Calgary or Vancouver. This guide walks you through everything you need to know in 2026, from grade equivalence and required documents to ESL programs and the Canada Child Benefit.

How the K-12 System Works in Canada

Canada has no federal department of education. Each of the ten provinces and three territories runs its own school system, sets its own curriculum, and licenses its own teachers. The structure most newcomer families recognize is K-12: kindergarten (sometimes split into junior and senior kindergarten in Ontario) through Grade 12. Quebec is the main exception, where students finish high school after Grade 11 and then attend CEGEP for two years before university.

Public education is free for children of permanent residents, Canadian citizens, and most temporary residents with a valid work or study permit. That includes your kids if you are in Canada on a closed or open work permit, or studying at a designated learning institution. Tourists and visitors usually cannot enroll children in public school without paying international student fees.

Children must attend school from age six or seven (depending on the province) until age 16 or 18. Kindergarten is optional in most provinces but offered tuition-free, and the vast majority of Canadian families take advantage of it.

The School Year and Calendar

The Canadian school year runs from early September to late June, with a two-week winter break around Christmas and a one-week March break (sometimes called spring break). Some provinces use a two-semester system at the high school level, where students take four courses per semester. Others use a full-year timetable with eight subjects spread across the year. British Columbia and Alberta high schools often run semestered, while Ontario typically uses semesters but with regional variation.

Knowing the calendar matters for one practical reason: if you arrive in July or August, you have time to register before September. If you arrive in November or February, you can still register, kids simply join their grade mid-year.

Public, Catholic, Private, and Independent Schools

Canada has four broad categories of schools, and the distinction confuses many newcomer parents.

The big surprise for many families: in Ontario, Alberta, and Saskatchewan, Catholic schools are part of the publicly funded system. They are tuition-free and follow the provincial curriculum, but include religious instruction. In other provinces, Catholic schools exist but charge tuition like any private school. We see families regularly assume "Catholic equals private," which is incorrect in the three provinces above.

Documents You Need to Register

Bring originals and one photocopy of each. Most school boards will scan and return originals on the spot.

  • Proof of the child's identity and age: passport, birth certificate (translated if not in English or French), or Canadian citizenship document
  • Proof of immigration status: PR card, COPR, work permit, study permit, or visitor record showing the child's status
  • Proof of address: rental lease, utility bill, property tax bill, or bank statement showing your Canadian address (this is what determines your school catchment)
  • Custody documents: if applicable, especially for separated or divorced parents
  • Immunization records: translated to English or French. Ontario, New Brunswick, and a few other provinces require proof of certain vaccines (DTaP, polio, MMR, meningococcal, varicella) for enrollment or continued attendance. Other provinces strongly recommend but do not strictly require them at registration.
  • Prior school transcripts or report cards: translated. Useful for placement, especially for older kids entering high school where credits may transfer.

If you don't have translated immunization records on hand, register anyway and submit the translation within the deadline the board gives you. Don't let missing paperwork delay registration, you can almost always start the process and finish documentation later.

ESL and EAL Support in Public Schools

If English is not your child's first language, the public school will assess their language level (sometimes called the STEP assessment in Ontario, or similar provincial frameworks elsewhere) and place them in an ESL or EAL (English as an Additional Language) stream. These programs are built into the regular school day at no extra cost. Students attend mainstream classes for some subjects and receive targeted language support for others.

You usually don't have to request ESL, the school identifies eligible kids during registration. But it's worth asking explicitly, because in some boards the assessment is automatic only if a parent flags the home language as non-English on the registration form. This is one of the most common oversights we see. Always check the home language box honestly and ask whether your child qualifies for language support.

For French-immersion families or those settling in Quebec, equivalent programs exist for French language acquisition (called francisation in Quebec).

Where to Actually Register

Registration happens at the local school board, not the federal or provincial government. Your address determines which board and which catchment school your child attends. Major boards include:

  • Toronto District School Board (TDSB) and the Toronto Catholic District School Board (TCDSB) for Toronto
  • Peel District School Board for Mississauga and Brampton
  • Calgary Board of Education and Calgary Catholic for Calgary
  • Vancouver School Board for Vancouver
  • Conseil scolaire de Montréal for francophone Montreal

Visit the board's website, find the "newcomer" or "international students" or "register your child" page, and follow the steps. Most boards have a dedicated welcome centre for newcomer families that handles assessment, document review, and placement in one appointment.

Mid-Year Enrollment

Yes, kids can start school any time of year. Most newcomer families have their children in class within a week or two of completing registration. The school board will not make you wait for September. If you arrive in January, your child joins their grade in January.

After-School Care and Programs

School days typically end around 3pm, which doesn't match a typical work schedule. After-school care (sometimes called "before and after care") is available at most elementary schools through the YMCA, municipal recreation departments, or private providers. Costs range from $300 to $700 per month per child, partially offset for many families by the federal $10-a-day child care program now rolling out across most provinces for kids under 6.

Older children often join extracurricular activities through the school itself (sports, music, clubs) or through community centres, which are usually low-cost or free with a city recreation pass.

The Canada Child Benefit (CCB)

The CCB is a tax-free monthly payment from the federal government to eligible families with children under 18. For the July 2025 to June 2026 benefit year, the maximum annual benefit is approximately $7,997 per child under age 6 and $6,748 per child aged 6 through 17. The amount is income-tested, so families earning above roughly $37,000 receive progressively less, but most newcomer families in their first year qualify for close to the maximum because their Canadian income is low.

To receive CCB you generally need to file a Canadian tax return (even with zero income) and apply through CRA. New permanent residents can apply within their first year using form RC66 plus the RC66SCH schedule for newcomers. Funds typically start arriving within 8-11 weeks of application.

Common Mistakes We See

After years of helping families settle, these are the slip-ups that come up most often:

  1. Assuming Catholic schools are private. In Ontario, Alberta, and Saskatchewan they are publicly funded and tuition-free.
  2. Showing up without translated immunization records. This delays registration in provinces that require them.
  3. Not requesting an ESL assessment. If you don't flag home language clearly, your child may be placed in mainstream classes without language support.
  4. Registering at the wrong board. Catchment is by exact address, not city. A move of three blocks can change schools.
  5. Waiting until September. You can register the day you arrive, and your child can start days later.
  6. Forgetting to apply for CCB. Even if you have no Canadian income yet, file a tax return and apply.

Appendix: Fact-Check Notes

  • CCB 2025-26 benefit year amounts: Maximum $7,997/year per child under 6, $6,748/year per child 6-17 (CRA, indexed annually each July). Confirmed for July 2025-June 2026 cycle.
  • Catholic publicly funded provinces: Ontario, Alberta, and Saskatchewan are the three provinces with constitutionally protected publicly funded separate (Catholic) school systems. Quebec abolished its denominational system in 1998. Newfoundland and Labrador did the same in 1997.
  • Immunization requirements: Ontario's Immunization of School Pupils Act (ISPA) requires DTaP, polio, MMR, meningococcal, and varicella (for kids born in 2010 or later). New Brunswick has similar mandates. Most other provinces strongly recommend but do not legally mandate vaccines for school entry. Quebec, BC, Alberta, and others rely on voluntary compliance.
  • School year: September to late June is the standard across all provinces. Specific start and end dates vary by board and year.
  • Grade placement by age: Canadian schools place by chronological age as of September 1 (or December 31 in some provinces) by default, with limited flexibility.
  • $10-a-day child care: Federal-provincial agreements rolling out across all provinces except Quebec (which has its own long-standing subsidized system). Coverage varies and waitlists are long in major cities.
