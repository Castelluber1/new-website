Foreign Credentials in Canada: ECA, Licensing, and the Reality of Working in Your Field in 2026

10 min read•2026-05-16

You arrive in Canada with a degree, a license, and ten years of experience. Then someone tells you that your credentials need to be "evaluated," "assessed," and possibly "recognized" by a provincial regulator before you can do the same job you did back home. Some newcomers land their first professional role in six months. Others spend three years driving Uber while studying for a board exam. The difference is almost never talent. It is almost always understanding the two parallel systems that govern foreign credentials in Canada, and planning around them before you land.

This guide separates the two things that get confused most often: the Educational Credential Assessment (ECA) used for immigration, and the professional licensing process used to actually work in a regulated profession. We cover timelines, costs, the top regulated professions, bridging programs, the survival-job reality, and the specific gotchas Brazilian applicants run into.

ECA vs professional licensing: two different systems, two different goals

The single most common mistake we see is treating an ECA report as a license to work. It is not. They serve different purposes and are issued by different organizations.

An Educational Credential Assessment (ECA) is an immigration document. It tells Immigration, Refugees and Citizenship Canada (IRCC) what your foreign degree is equivalent to in the Canadian education system, so they can award you points under Express Entry or confirm eligibility for the Federal Skilled Worker program. An ECA does not let you practice medicine, engineering, or law in Canada. It just confirms your education on paper.

Professional licensing is a provincial process. Each regulated profession in Canada has a college or association in every province that decides who can call themselves a nurse, engineer, teacher, lawyer, or accountant. They review your education, your experience, sometimes additional coursework, and almost always one or more exams. They issue the license. Without it, you legally cannot use the title or perform the regulated work.

You can hold an ECA and still not be able to work in your field. You can also work in many fields in Canada with no ECA and no license, because most jobs are not regulated.

The 5 IRCC-designated ECA organizations

For immigration purposes, IRCC currently designates five general-purpose ECA providers:

  • WES — World Education Services
  • IQAS — International Qualifications Assessment Service (Government of Alberta)
  • ICAS — International Credential Assessment Service of Canada
  • CES — Comparative Education Service (University of Toronto)
  • ICES — International Credential Evaluation Service (BCIT)

Two additional organizations are designated only for specific regulated professions: the Medical Council of Canada (MCC) for physicians, and the Pharmacy Examining Board of Canada (PEBC) for pharmacists. If you are a doctor or pharmacist applying for Express Entry, IRCC requires you to use the regulator instead of a general ECA agency.

For a deep comparison of fees, timelines, and country coverage across the five general agencies, see our breakdown of ECA for Express Entry: WES vs IQAS vs ICAS vs CES. For the general overview of how ECAs fit into the PR process, see Educational Credential Assessment.

The short version: WES is the most popular, ICES is one of the fastest, and IQAS is usually the cheapest but the slowest. Pick based on country of study and how soon you need the report, not on what a forum told you.

Regulated vs non-regulated professions

In Canada, roughly 20% of jobs are regulated. That means about one in five occupations legally requires a license from a provincial body before you can practice. The other 80% are non-regulated. For non-regulated roles, employers decide what to recognize, and your foreign degree plus experience can be enough on day one.

Whether your profession is regulated depends on the province. Nursing is regulated everywhere. Project management is regulated nowhere. Some occupations, like early childhood education, are regulated in some provinces and not others. The Government of Canada Job Bank lets you search occupations by NOC code and province to see if a license is required.

If your field is non-regulated, your job hunt is closer to a normal job hunt, plus the credential-translation step. If your field is regulated, you need to plan the licensing pathway before you land, because some pathways take years.

Top regulated professions and their pathways

Here is the overview of the most common regulated professions for newcomers, with the national or provincial body that controls licensing.

Pharmacist Pharmacy Examining Board of Canada (PEBC) PEBC Evaluating Exam, Qualifying Exam Parts I & II 2-4 years $8,000-$15,000
Registered Nurse National Nursing Assessment Service (NNAS) + provincial college NCLEX-RN 1-3 years $3,000-$8,000
Engineer (P.Eng.) Engineers Canada + provincial association (e.g., PEO in Ontario) Technical exams (often waived), Professional Practice Exam 1-3 years $1,500-$4,000
Teacher (K-12) Provincial College of Teachers (e.g., Ontario College of Teachers) Varies by province; sometimes additional coursework 6-18 months $500-$2,500
Lawyer Provincial Law Society + Federation of Law Societies (NCA) NCA exams, bar exam, articling 2-4 years $10,000-$20,000
Accountant (CPA) CPA Canada + provincial CPA body CPA Professional Education Program, Common Final Examination 2-4 years $10,000-$20,000

Costs include exam fees, document evaluation, and registration but exclude prep courses, relocation for residency, and lost income during study. Always verify current fees on each regulator's website.

Physicians

Internationally educated physicians go through the Medical Council of Canada. You start by getting your medical credential source-verified through physiciansapply.ca, then write the MCCQE Part I (the qualifying exam) and the NAC OSCE (a clinical skills assessment). The hardest step is not the exams; it is matching into a residency through CaRMS, where international medical graduates compete for a limited number of seats. Some provinces have practice-ready assessment routes for family medicine that skip residency, which can shorten the timeline significantly.

Pharmacists

PEBC controls pharmacist licensing nationally (except in Quebec). You begin with document evaluation, then write the Evaluating Examination, then Qualifying Examination Parts I and II. You also need to complete a structured practical training program in most provinces. Budget two to four years from landing to license.

Nurses

The pathway runs through the National Nursing Assessment Service (NNAS), which creates an advisory report sent to the provincial nursing college you apply to. The college then decides whether you need bridging education, additional clinical hours, or you can sit directly for the NCLEX-RN. The NCLEX-RN registration fee is USD $200 through Pearson VUE, plus provincial college fees. See our piece on Express Entry for nurses for the immigration side of the same journey.

Engineers

Engineers Canada coordinates national standards, but licensing is provincial. In Ontario you apply to PEO (Professional Engineers Ontario); in BC, to EGBC; in Alberta, to APEGA. Most international engineers from accredited programs do not need to rewrite technical exams; you almost always need the Professional Practice Exam and you need to document Canadian-equivalent work experience. Engineering is one of the more accessible regulated pathways.

Teachers

K-12 teachers apply to the provincial college of teachers in their target province. The Ontario College of Teachers is the largest. You submit your transcripts, teaching credential, and references, and the college decides whether to grant a certificate of qualification, require additional courses, or both. Teachers from English-speaking countries with similar K-12 systems usually have the smoothest path.

Lawyers

Foreign-trained lawyers go through the Federation of Law Societies of Canada's National Committee on Accreditation (NCA). The NCA reviews your law degree and assigns required exams (typically 5-10). After passing, you apply to the provincial law society, complete articling or the LPP, and write the bar exam. It is a long, expensive process, and many internationally educated lawyers end up working as paralegals or in policy roles instead.

Accountants

The Chartered Professional Accountants of Canada (CPA Canada) sets national standards; provincial CPA bodies handle licensing. International accountants get a transcript assessment, may receive exemptions from some modules of the CPA Professional Education Program (PEP), and must write the Common Final Examination (CFE). Several countries have mutual recognition agreements (the US, Australia, Ireland, Mexico, Hong Kong) that significantly shorten the pathway. Brazil does not.

Bridging programs: the most under-used resource

Most provinces fund bridging programs specifically for internationally educated professionals. These programs combine coursework, language support, mentorship, and supervised practice to get you from "foreign credential" to "ready for the licensing exam" faster than going it alone.

Examples:

  • CARE for Nurses (Ontario) — bridging for internationally educated nurses preparing for NCLEX-RN
  • Internationally Educated Engineers Qualification (IEEQ) programs — offered through several universities
  • Internationally Educated Pharmacists Bridging Program (IPG) at the University of Toronto
  • Internationally Trained Lawyers Program (ITLP) at the University of Toronto

Bridging programs are often free or heavily subsidized for permanent residents. Some are open to work permit holders too. Search "[your profession] bridging program [your province]" or check the provincial Ministry of Labour website. This is the single highest-leverage resource most newcomers miss.

The survival job reality

Here is the uncomfortable truth: most internationally educated professionals in regulated fields work outside their profession for the first one to three years. Doctors driving for Uber and engineers stocking warehouse shelves is a real Canadian phenomenon. It does not mean the system is broken, and it does not mean you failed. It means the licensing process takes time and you have rent to pay.

The right strategy is not to fight this. It is to plan for it.

  • Pick a survival job that pays the bills without consuming all your study time. Night-shift roles, weekend work, and gig flexibility are friends here.
  • Look for adjacent roles that count as Canadian experience in your field: a foreign-trained nurse can work as a personal support worker (PSW); a foreign engineer can work as an engineering technologist or CAD operator; a foreign lawyer can work as a paralegal.
  • Use the survival-job period to complete the licensing steps in parallel: document evaluation, language tests, exam prep, networking.

Your goal is to compress the survival-job phase, not to avoid it.

Federal strategy for internationally educated professionals

The federal government has been increasing investment in foreign credential recognition. The Foreign Credential Recognition Program (FCRP), administered by Employment and Social Development Canada (ESDC), funds provincial regulators, bridging programs, and loan programs to help newcomers get licensed faster. The federal Foreign Credential Recognition Loan Program provides loans of up to $30,000 (the amount and terms vary by delivery partner) for licensing fees, exam costs, course tuition, and living expenses while you complete the process.

For 2025-2026, ongoing federal priorities include faster recognition pathways for healthcare workers (nurses, physicians, pharmacists) and improved data sharing between provinces. If you are a healthcare professional, this works in your favour. Check the ESDC website and your provincial counterpart for the most current programs.

Brazil-specific notes

A few things matter specifically for Brazilian applicants:

  • Portuguese-language credentials need certified translation. Every ECA agency and every regulator requires English or French translations. Use a certified translator — see our guide on tradução juramentada para o Canadá. For document apostille, see apostilamento de documentos brasileiros.
  • Medicine and dentistry are the hardest pathways. Brazilian medical and dental schools are not on the short list of programs with streamlined recognition. Plan for the long route — typically 4-7 years to a full license, including residency.
  • Engineering and IT are the smoothest. Brazilian engineering programs from CREA-recognized institutions are well-regarded by provincial associations. For software roles, most positions are not regulated at all — you can start work as soon as you land. See our piece on Express Entry for software engineers (NOC 21232).
  • Accounting, law, and pharmacy are middle of the road. Long but well-defined pathways. CPA, NCA, and PEBC are all doable; budget 2-4 years.
  • Nursing is high-demand and well-supported. Brazilian COREN-registered nurses have a clear NNAS path, and several provinces actively recruit foreign nurses with bridging programs and signing incentives.

One more thing: do not under-invest in your resume and LinkedIn. Canadian employers read both very differently than Brazilian recruiters do. Format, language, and structure matter. See 5 mistakes newcomers make on their resume before you start applying.

A realistic plan for your first 18 months

  1. Months 0-3 (before landing): Start your ECA, get certified translations of all transcripts and licenses, take IELTS or CELPIP, research the regulator in your target province.
  2. Months 3-6 (first months in Canada): File your provincial licensing application. Apply to bridging programs. Find a survival job that protects study time.
  3. Months 6-12: Complete required exams or coursework. Network through your professional association. Look for adjacent roles in your field.
  4. Months 12-18: Sit for licensing exams. Apply to your first licensed role.

This compresses what often becomes a three-year drift into a structured plan. Adjust the timeline to your profession — physicians and lawyers will be longer, engineers and teachers can be faster.
