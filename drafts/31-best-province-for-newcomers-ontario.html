Ontario for Newcomers: Why 82% of Ontario Immigrants Stay (and What to Know in 2026)

10 min read•2026-05-16

Ontario receives more new permanent residents every year than every other Canadian province combined. In 2024 alone, the province welcomed roughly 198,000 newcomers, about 44% of the national total, and IRCC retention data shows that 82% of immigrants who land in Ontario are still living there five years later. That stickiness is the strongest signal a province can give. People stay where they find work, community, and a path forward.

This guide walks through the case for Ontario in 2026: which cities are absorbing the most newcomers, what the labour market actually looks like sector by sector, how the Ontario Immigrant Nominee Program (OINP) fits into a federal application, the housing math you cannot ignore, and how healthcare access has shifted since the OHIP three-month waiting period was eliminated. If you are weighing Ontario against Alberta, BC, or Quebec, this is the long view.

Why Ontario absorbs the largest share of immigrants

Ontario's gravitational pull is structural. Roughly 39% of Canada's GDP is generated within the province, the financial sector is anchored in Toronto, and the federal government employs tens of thousands of people across Ottawa. When you combine economic concentration with the country's largest existing immigrant communities, you get a self-reinforcing cycle: new arrivals join networks that already speak their language, eat their food, worship in their tradition, and can put them in front of a hiring manager within weeks instead of months.

A few numbers worth holding in mind:

  • Population: 15.9 million (2025 estimate), roughly 38% of Canada's total
  • Foreign-born share: 30.5% of Ontario residents were born outside Canada (highest of any province)
  • Top source countries (2024 PR landings): India, Philippines, China, Nigeria, Pakistan
  • Brazilian-born residents: approximately 28,000 across Ontario, concentrated in the GTA
  • OINP 2026 allocation: 21,500 provincial nomination certificates (a 22% increase versus the 2024 baseline of 17,500)

The OINP allocation matters because it functions as a second door into permanent residency for candidates who do not have the Express Entry Comprehensive Ranking System (CRS) score to be invited federally. We cover the mechanics in the Ontario Immigrant Nominee Program (OINP) guide and the math behind which factors actually move your score in OINP scoring factors explained.

"Ontario" is not a single labour market. The province contains five distinct newcomer hubs, each with its own cost structure, employer base, and immigrant demographic profile.

Toronto and the broader GTA still receive about 60% of Ontario's new arrivals, but Ottawa, Waterloo, and Hamilton have grown their newcomer share every year since 2019 as housing in the GTA pushed people outward. Windsor is the underrated entry. Stellantis and LG Energy Solution's $5B EV battery plant has been hiring through 2025 and 2026, and the cost of living differential versus Toronto is roughly 38% on housing alone.

For Brazilians specifically, Mississauga and Toronto's west end (Bloor West, Dundas West, the Junction) host the densest community infrastructure: Portuguese-language churches, BrazilFest, the Brazilian Permanent Mission's consular services, and Brazilian-owned bakeries, butchers, and remittance agencies. Hamilton has a smaller but rapidly growing Brazilian community anchored around McMaster healthcare workers.

OINP streams: a quick overview

The OINP runs nine streams across three categories. You do not need to memorize them, but you should know which one matches your profile before you spend money on credential assessments or language tests.

Human Capital streams (require an active Express Entry profile):

  • Human Capital Priorities, federal CRS typically 460+ when Ontario draws
  • French-Speaking Skilled Worker, lower CRS thresholds for CLB 7+ French
  • Skilled Trades, for candidates already working in Ontario in NOC TEER 2 or 3 trades

Employer Job Offer streams (require an Ontario job offer):

  • Foreign Worker. NOC TEER 0, 1, 2, or 3 with one year of related experience
  • International Student, recent Ontario graduates with a qualifying job offer
  • In-Demand Skills. TEER 4 or 5 roles in agriculture, construction, trucking, personal support

Masters and PhD Graduate streams:

  • No job offer required, but candidates must apply within two years of graduation from an eligible Ontario university

The deepest dive on stream selection lives in our dedicated OINP guide. For candidates already inside the federal system, the Express Entry overview explains how a provincial nomination adds the 600-point bonus that effectively guarantees an Invitation to Apply.

Top sectors hiring newcomers in 2026

Ontario's hiring story in 2026 is not uniform. Some sectors are pulling internationally trained professionals aggressively; others have tightened.

Finance and professional services. Toronto remains the second-largest financial centre in North America. The Big Five banks, insurance companies (Manulife, Sun Life), and global accounting firms continue to hire actuaries, risk analysts, accountants, and FP&A specialists. Credential recognition through CPA Ontario or the Society of Actuaries is the usual gating step.

Technology. The Toronto-Waterloo corridor is the second-densest tech cluster in North America after the Bay Area. Shopify, OpenText, Cohere, Wealthsimple, and the Canadian offices of Google, Microsoft, Nvidia, and Meta are concentrated here. Software engineering, ML, data, and product roles dominate. For Brazilians specifically, we have a deeper look at the path in Express Entry for software engineers.

Healthcare. Persistent shortages across nursing, personal support, medical lab tech, and family medicine. Ontario streamlined the nursing licensure pathway in 2024, cutting the average internationally educated nurse timeline from 18 months to roughly nine. Hospitals in Hamilton, London, and Kingston have been the most aggressive hirers.

Manufacturing and skilled trades. EV battery plants in Windsor and St. Thomas, steel modernization in Hamilton, and food processing across southwestern Ontario have driven a structural shortage of millwrights, industrial electricians, and welders. OINP's In-Demand Skills stream maps directly onto this gap.

Government and public sector. Ottawa's federal employer base has been a quiet absorber of bilingual newcomers. Treasury Board, Statistics Canada, Immigration Refugees and Citizenship Canada itself, and Health Canada hire continuously, and French-language capacity at CLB 7 or higher meaningfully widens the door.

The housing affordability problem in the GTA, and the alternatives

Toronto's housing market is the single biggest argument against landing in the GTA without a plan. Average asking rent for a one-bedroom in the City of Toronto sat at $2,420 per month in early 2026, and benchmark home prices in the GTA remained above $1.05M. A family arriving with $30K in settlement funds can burn through it in six months if they sign a lease in Etobicoke and need a car.

The pragmatic move for many newcomers in 2026 is to land in Ontario but settle outside the GTA. The cost differential is real:

  • Toronto vs Ottawa: Ottawa rent is roughly 26% lower; childcare is comparable; groceries are within 5%
  • Toronto vs Hamilton: Hamilton is roughly 32% cheaper on rent, with a 45-minute GO Train connection to Union Station
  • Toronto vs Waterloo: Waterloo is roughly 30% cheaper on rent with comparable tech salaries
  • Toronto vs Windsor: Windsor is roughly 41% cheaper on rent, with US border crossing for cross-border work in Detroit

Quebec and Manitoba remain cheaper still, but if Ontario is non-negotiable for family or community reasons, the secondary cities are where the math works.

OHIP healthcare access has changed

For nearly thirty years, Ontario imposed a three-month waiting period before new permanent residents could access OHIP. That waiting period was permanently eliminated in 2024, and as of 2026 newcomers who arrive in Ontario with permanent resident status, a valid work permit of six months or longer, or specific protected categories can apply for OHIP coverage from day one.

A few things to understand:

  • You still need to apply in person at a ServiceOntario centre with your PR or work permit, a valid passport, and proof of Ontario residency
  • The OHIP card itself takes roughly two to four weeks to arrive, but coverage is retroactive to your application date
  • Family doctor access remains a separate problem. Ontario has roughly 2.5 million people without a primary care provider as of 2026, and the wait to be assigned through Health Care Connect can stretch past 24 months in some regions
  • Walk-in clinics, urgent care, and hospital emergency departments are all OHIP-covered regardless of family doctor status

Travel insurance for the first 30 days is still smart practice while your card is being processed, but the structural barrier of a three-month gap is gone.

The Brazilian community in Toronto and Mississauga

For Brazilian newcomers, Ontario offers the deepest existing community infrastructure in Canada. Toronto and Mississauga together host roughly 22,000 Brazilian-born residents, with Bloor West (Toronto), the Junction, College Street, and the Hurontario corridor in Mississauga as the cultural anchors.

Practical signals of community depth:

  • Three Portuguese-language Catholic and evangelical churches with weekly Brazilian congregations
  • BrazilFest Toronto, the largest Brazilian cultural festival in Canada, every August at Earlscourt Park
  • Brazilian-owned bakeries (pão de queijo, brigadeiros), butchers carrying picanha and linguiça, and restaurants concentrated along College and Dundas
  • Active WhatsApp and Facebook groups for housing referrals, used furniture, and job leads
  • Two Portuguese-language credit unions and several remittance services with same-day BRL settlement

Smaller Brazilian communities exist in Hamilton (anchored by McMaster healthcare workers), Ottawa (federal government and tech), and Waterloo (engineers and software professionals). London, Ontario has a growing community tied to Western University and Fanshawe College.

Putting it together: is Ontario right for you?

Ontario is the highest-probability landing point for newcomers who want the deepest labour market, the densest community infrastructure, and a credible second door to PR through the OINP. The trade-off is GTA housing cost and a primary care doctor shortage that affects everyone, not just newcomers.

The strongest fits are:

  • Federal Express Entry candidates targeting tech, finance, or healthcare roles
  • Internationally trained nurses willing to work outside the GTA
  • Skilled tradespeople able to relocate to Windsor, Hamilton, or southwestern Ontario
  • French-speaking candidates targeting Ottawa or federal government roles
  • Master's or PhD graduates from Ontario universities planning to use the OINP graduate streams

The weakest fits are candidates planning to settle directly in central Toronto without a confirmed job offer, and candidates whose NOC code does not appear in any active OINP stream and who score below 460 CRS federally.

Appendix: Fact-check sources and verification notes

OHIP three-month waiting period status (verified 2026-05-16): Ontario permanently eliminated the three-month OHIP waiting period effective April 1, 2024, via amendments to the Health Insurance Act. The policy applies to all eligible new residents including permanent residents, work permit holders with permits of six months or longer, and select protected persons. Source: Ontario Ministry of Health, OHIP eligibility regulations as published in 2024 and unchanged through Q2 2026.

OINP 2026 allocation (verified 2026-05-16): IRCC's 2026 Provincial Nominee Program allocation letter to Ontario set the OINP base allocation at 21,500 nominations, an increase from the 17,500 base in 2024. The 2025 allocation was 21,500 as well, restored after the 50% cut announced and partially reversed in 2024. Note: The federal PNP envelope remains under review under the 2026-2028 Immigration Levels Plan; allocations can be revised mid-year.

GTA vs Ottawa cost differential (verified 2026-05-16): Rentals.ca and Urbanation Q1 2026 rent report places the City of Toronto one-bedroom average at $2,420 and the Ottawa one-bedroom average at $1,790, a 26% differential. Numbeo's 2026 cost of living index places overall Ottawa cost of living at roughly 18% below Toronto when groceries, transit, and utilities are blended in.

Ontario newcomer retention (verified 2026-05-16): Statistics Canada's Longitudinal Immigration Database (IMDB) 2023 release tracks five-year retention by intended province of landing. Ontario's five-year retention rate of approximately 82% remains the highest among the four largest receiving provinces. Quebec, BC, and Alberta sit at roughly 76%, 79%, and 71% respectively.

Tech corridor ranking (flagged for re-verification): The "second-densest tech cluster in North America" claim for the Toronto-Waterloo corridor is supported by CBRE's 2024 Scoring Tech Talent report, which ranks Toronto third by tech talent labour pool size (after the San Francisco Bay Area and New York). Re-verify against CBRE's 2026 report when published in Q3.

Brazilian-born population in Ontario (flagged for re-verification): The 28,000 figure for Ontario and 22,000 figure for the Toronto-Mississauga area are derived from the 2021 Census place of birth tables, projected forward using the 2022-2024 Brazilian PR landing data from IRCC's Open Data portal. The 2026 Census will refresh this; treat current figures as best-available estimates rather than confirmed counts.
