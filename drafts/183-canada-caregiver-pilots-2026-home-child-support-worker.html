Canada Caregiver Pilots 2026: Home Child Care + Home Support Worker Pathways to PR

10 min read•2026-05-17

If you care for children, seniors, or people who need help recovering at home, Canada has built two dedicated immigration programs around your skills. The Home Child Care Provider Pilot and the Home Support Worker Pilot, usually referred to together as the Caregiver Pilots, give qualified caregivers a structured route to permanent residence, with the option to start working in Canada before your PR application is finalized.

These pilots have evolved a lot since 2019, and the current iteration (the "enhanced" Caregiver Pilots launched in March 2024) made the eligibility bar lower in some places and higher in others. If you are planning your move in 2026, the rules you read on older blogs may no longer apply. This guide walks through how the programs work today, who qualifies, the two routes to PR, the annual cap, the family benefits, and what to do before applications open each year.

What are the Canada Caregiver Pilots?

The Caregiver Pilots are two separate but nearly identical permanent residence programs run by Immigration, Refugees and Citizenship Canada (IRCC):

  1. Home Child Care Provider Pilot (HCCPP), for people who care for children under 18 in a private home. Maps to NOC 44100.
  2. Home Support Worker Pilot (HSWP), for people who provide personal care to seniors, people with disabilities, or those recovering from illness or injury in a private home. Maps to NOC 44101.

Both occupations sit at TEER 4 under the National Occupational Classification (NOC) 2021 system. That matters because TEER 4 jobs are not eligible for Express Entry's main streams in most cases, so the Caregiver Pilots are essentially the dedicated PR door for this workforce.

The current pilots were re-opened on March 27, 2024 as "enhanced" versions, and IRCC has signalled they will be replaced by permanent caregiver programs once the design work is done. Until that transition happens, the pilots are the route.

The two pathways to PR

This is the part most applicants get wrong. There is not one application; there are two ways in, depending on whether you already have Canadian caregiving experience or not.

Pathway Who it is for Work permit needed first? Time to PR
Gaining Experience You have a valid full-time job offer in Canada as a caregiver but do not yet have 12 months of Canadian caregiving work experience. Yes, occupation-specific work permit issued with PR application. After 12 months of qualifying Canadian work experience, you submit the second-stage PR documents.
Direct to PR You already have at least 12 months of full-time Canadian work experience in the eligible NOC (44100 or 44101) in the last 36 months. No, you go straight to PR. Standard PR processing once the application is complete and accepted into the cap.

Both pathways lead to the same outcome: permanent residence for you and your accompanying family members. The difference is whether you need to build the experience in Canada first or whether you bring it with you.

Gaining Experience pathway in practice

If you are applying from outside Canada (or you are in Canada on a different status), the Gaining Experience route is usually the realistic path. You need a genuine, full-time, indeterminate job offer from a Canadian employer in a private home, not an agency, not a daycare, not a long-term care facility. IRCC issues an occupation-specific open work permit tied to the caregiver NOC. You then work, accumulate 12 months of qualifying experience within 36 months, and submit your experience documentation to complete the PR.

Direct to PR pathway in practice

If you are already in Canada, for example, as a caregiver under a previous program, a live-in caregiver, or on another work permit where you happened to perform NOC 44100 or 44101 duties, and you can document 12 months of full-time work in the last 36 months, you skip the work permit step and apply for PR directly. This is faster but the experience bar is real: hours, employer letters, pay records all need to line up.

Eligibility requirements

The requirements are intentionally accessible, but they are strict in the sense that missing one means a refusal. Here is the current baseline:

Requirement Detail
Language CLB/NCLC 5 in all four skills (reading, writing, listening, speaking). Approved tests include IELTS General, CELPIP General, TEF Canada, and TCF Canada. Results valid for 2 years at submission.
Education A Canadian post-secondary credential of at least 1 year, OR a foreign credential of equivalent value supported by an Educational Credential Assessment (ECA) from a designated organization.
Work experience Direct to PR: 12 months of full-time Canadian experience in NOC 44100 or 44101 within the last 36 months. Gaining Experience: a valid full-time job offer in one of those NOCs.
Job offer (if applicable) Full-time (30+ hours/week), in a private home, from a Canadian employer who is not an embassy, high commission, or consulate. LMIA not required under the pilots.
Admissibility Medical exam, police certificates from every country lived in for 6+ months since age 18, and biometrics.
Settlement funds (Gaining Experience only) Proof of funds based on family size, unless you are already legally working in Canada.

A few clarifications that trip people up:

  • "Full-time" means 30 hours per week minimum, regardless of how many employers, though the Caregiver Pilots favour a single full-time employer.
  • The job offer must be for work in a private home. Group homes, retirement residences, and hospitals do not qualify under these pilots.
  • Volunteer hours, unpaid internships, and self-employment do not count toward the 12 months.
  • The post-secondary education requirement (1+ year) is genuinely enforced, high school alone is not enough, even with years of experience.

The annual cap: 5,500 + 5,500

Each pilot has its own annual intake cap. For 2025, IRCC accepted 5,500 PR applications in each pilot (so 11,000 in total across the two streams), and the same cap structure has carried into 2026. People often round this up to "about 6,000 invites per year per stream" in conversation, but the formal published number is 5,500 per pilot.

The cap is split between the two pathways. Historically, IRCC has reserved roughly half the spaces for Gaining Experience applicants (those applying with a job offer from abroad or from inside Canada without 12 months experience yet) and roughly half for Direct to PR applicants. The intake opens on a set date each year and tends to fill in days, sometimes hours, especially on the Gaining Experience side. Preparation matters more than speed of clicking, if your file is not complete on the day the portal opens, you lose your spot.

Who can come with you

This is one of the most important features of the Caregiver Pilots and one of the biggest differences compared to programs that came before. You can include:

  • Your spouse or common-law partner. They receive an open work permit, meaning they can work for almost any Canadian employer in any role.
  • Your dependent children. Children under 22 (or older if financially dependent due to a disability) can be included and receive study permits for K-12 public schooling, generally at no cost in most provinces.

This is what makes the pilots a true family pathway, not just a worker pathway. You arrive together, work and study together, and become permanent residents together.

Step-by-step: applying through the Gaining Experience pathway

  1. Confirm the NOC. Read NOC 44100 (Home Child Care Provider) and NOC 44101 (Home Support Worker) carefully. The duties on the job offer must clearly match.
  2. Take a language test. Aim for CLB 5 minimum, but score higher if you can. Higher scores help you in future programs if the pilots ever close.
  3. Get your ECA. If your education is from outside Canada, order an ECA report from a designated organization. This takes 4 to 20 weeks depending on the issuer and the country of education.
  4. Secure a qualifying job offer. Private home, full-time, indeterminate, written, and signed by both parties.
  5. Prepare your PR application package with the work permit application included. IRCC processes them together once the intake opens.
  6. Submit on opening day when IRCC re-opens the intake. Files are reviewed in the order received.
  7. Work for 12 months in Canada in the qualifying NOC.
  8. Submit your second-stage PR documents confirming the experience. PR is finalized after review.

Common reasons applications fail

Working through this for the last three years, the pattern of refusals is remarkably consistent:

  • Job offer for the wrong workplace. A retirement residence is not a private home. An assisted living building managed by a corporation is not a private home. If the worksite is not a single-family residence, the offer likely does not qualify.
  • Education credential below the bar. A 6-month certificate does not equal a 1-year post-secondary credential. An ECA marked as "high school equivalent" does not satisfy the post-secondary requirement.
  • Language test under CLB 5. Many applicants score CLB 5 in three skills but CLB 4 in writing or speaking. The minimum is across all four.
  • Hours below 30/week. Part-time experience, even if it adds up to many calendar months, does not count.
  • Missing employer documentation. Pay stubs that do not match the T4, Records of Employment that show fewer hours than claimed, or employer letters without the required content.

How the pilots fit with other PR programs

The Caregiver Pilots are not your only option, but they are usually the most direct for people in caregiving occupations. Worth knowing:

  • Express Entry's Category-Based Selection has, in recent rounds, included a "healthcare and social services occupations" category that now covers some caregiving-adjacent NOCs. Some caregivers can build an Express Entry profile in parallel, even at TEER 4, if they hold an additional credential that maps to a higher TEER occupation.
  • Provincial Nominee Programs (PNPs), particularly in BC and Ontario, occasionally open caregiver-specific or health-sector streams. These move quickly and close without notice.
  • Atlantic Immigration Program can also be a fit for some caregiving roles if the employer is in Atlantic Canada and is a designated employer.

Generally, if you have a caregiver job offer in a private home, the Caregiver Pilot is the cleanest route. If you have a job offer in an institutional setting, the pilots will not work and you need to look at PNPs or Express Entry instead. What to do right now if you are planning to apply in 2026

If the next intake matters to you, treat the next few months as preparation, not waiting:

  1. Book your language test in the next 60 days. If you score below CLB 5, you have time to study and retake.
  2. Start your ECA today if you have not already. This is the longest-pole item.
  3. Audit your employer offer (if you have one) against NOC 44100 or 44101 duties and the "private home" requirement.
  4. Pull your work history from every country, with employer letters showing hours, duties, and dates.
  5. Get your police certificates moving, some countries take months to issue them.
  6. Build your settlement funds to the level required for your family size, unless you are already legally working in Canada.

Missing intake day with an incomplete file is the single most common reason qualified caregivers do not make the cap. The fix is preparation.

Bottom line

The Canada Caregiver Pilots are one of the most family-friendly PR pathways in the Canadian immigration system. They reward people whose work directly supports Canadian families, caring for children, seniors, and people recovering at home, with a clear route to permanent residence for themselves and their families.

The bar is real: CLB 5 in all four skills, a 1-year post-secondary credential, a genuine private-home job offer, and a clean, complete file. But for the people who meet that bar, this is one of the few PR programs in Canada where TEER 4 work is welcomed as a long-term contribution to the country.
