Job Offer for Express Entry: LMIA vs LMIA-Exempt CRS Points in 2026

10 min read•2026-05-16

A Canadian job offer is one of the few levers that can move a stagnant Express Entry profile by 50 or even 200 CRS points in a single update. For candidates sitting 30 to 80 points below recent cutoffs, that swing is often the difference between watching draws pass and receiving an Invitation to Apply (ITA).

But "get a job offer" is not a strategy. The rules around what counts as a valid job offer for Comprehensive Ranking System purposes are narrow, the underlying LMIA process can take 3 to 6 months and cost the employer real money, and the LMIA-exempt categories that still confer CRS points are a specific subset of the broader International Mobility Program (IMP) work permit categories.

This guide breaks down exactly how job-offer CRS points work in 2026, when they're worth pursuing, and the common pitfalls that cause otherwise solid candidates to lose the points they thought they had.

Why a job offer matters: 50 or 200 CRS points changes everything

Under the current CRS scoring tables, a qualifying Canadian job offer adds either 50 or 200 additional CRS points, depending on the NOC TEER level of the offered position.

To put that in context: in mid-2025, general Express Entry cutoffs hovered in the 520 to 540 range. A candidate sitting at 480 with a Bachelor's degree, CLB 9, and three years of foreign work experience is realistically out of reach for general draws without something changing. A 50-point job offer pushes that same candidate to 530, which is close to the cutoff zone. A 200-point offer pushes them to 680, which is above every general draw recorded since the system began.

That's the mechanic in one sentence: a job offer is the only CRS lever besides a Provincial Nominee Program (PNP) nomination that can produce a triple-digit point jump from a single change.

There is one critical caveat that has changed the strategic calculus since 2025. IRCC temporarily removed the CRS points for job offers in March 2025 as part of the broader response to LMIA fraud and integrity concerns (more on this in the 2024-2025 changes section below). At the time of writing in mid-2026, the rules around arranged employment points have been in flux and candidates need to verify the current state on IRCC's website before building a strategy around them. The framework below describes how the points work when they are in effect.

The +50 vs +200 split: which NOC TEER level earns what

The split is straightforward but consistently misunderstood:

Offered position (NOC TEER) CRS points (when arranged employment points are in effect)
TEER 0 — Senior management (NOC codes starting with 00, e.g. 00010 Legislators, 00012 Senior government managers, 00013 Senior managers — financial, communications and other business services) 200
TEER 0 (non-senior management), TEER 1, TEER 2, TEER 3 50
TEER 4 and TEER 5 0 (do not qualify)

The 200-point tier is genuinely narrow. It covers the senior-management NOC codes in the "00" range, not every TEER 0 occupation. A General Practice Manager or Restaurant Manager is TEER 0 but not in the 00-senior-management band, so the offer would be worth 50, not 200.

Most candidates pursuing arranged employment land in the 50-point tier: software engineers, registered nurses, accountants, chefs, electricians, truck drivers in supervisory roles, and most other skilled workers.

What counts as a "valid" job offer for CRS purposes

IRCC applies a narrow definition. A job offer earns arranged employment points only if it meets all of the following:

  1. Full-time — at least 30 paid hours per week.
  2. Non-seasonal — continuous, year-round work, not tied to a specific season or short project.
  3. At least one year in duration — if the offer is supported by an LMIA, the position must be for at least one year of continuous full-time work after you receive permanent residence. For LMIA-exempt offers, you must have already worked for the employer for at least one year of full-time skilled work on the qualifying work permit.
  4. In writing — a formal written offer letter signed by the employer, with the position, salary, hours, duties, and start date specified.
  5. In a TEER 0, 1, 2, or 3 occupation — confirmed by NOC code matching the duties.
  6. Backed by either an LMIA or a qualifying LMIA-exempt work permit — not every work permit qualifies; see the IMP section below.

A common rejection scenario: the candidate is on a Work Permit that does qualify for CRS points (for example, an intra-company transferee permit), but the actual job offer letter is sloppy, missing the duration commitment, or the duties don't match the claimed NOC. The officer concludes the offer doesn't meet the definition and the 50 points come off.

LMIA process: who applies, employer requirements, processing time

A Labour Market Impact Assessment (LMIA) is an Employment and Social Development Canada (ESDC) document confirming that hiring a foreign worker will not negatively affect the Canadian labour market. The employer applies for it, not the candidate. That distinction matters because the entire cost and administrative burden falls on the employer.

Core LMIA mechanics:

  • Fee: CAD $1,000 per position, paid by the employer (cannot be passed to the worker).
  • Processing time: typically 3 to 6 months for standard LMIAs, faster for Global Talent Stream cases.
  • Advertising requirement: the employer must advertise the position to Canadians and permanent residents for a minimum period (currently 4 weeks across at least three sources) before applying.
  • Wage requirement: the offered wage must meet or exceed the prevailing wage for the occupation in that region, published by ESDC.
  • Genuineness review: ESDC verifies the employer is operating in good faith, has the financial capacity to pay the wage, and is not on the non-compliant employer list.

For Express Entry purposes specifically, the LMIA needs to be "PR-supporting" — that is, issued for the purpose of supporting a permanent residence application, not for a closed work permit. An employer can request a dual-intent LMIA that supports both a work permit and PR, but they have to know to ask for it.

LMIA exemptions (when no LMIA is needed)

Not every foreign worker in Canada needs an LMIA. Workers under the International Mobility Program (IMP) are LMIA-exempt because their work permit falls under one of the categories where Canada has determined hiring a foreign national serves a broader policy interest. Some of these IMP categories still grant CRS points; many do not.

LMIA-exempt categories (IMP) that still grant CRS points

This is the most-misunderstood area of the entire job-offer framework. Holding an LMIA-exempt work permit does not automatically earn CRS points. Only specific IMP categories qualify, and the work permit must have been issued under one of those qualifying categories with at least one year of full-time skilled work already completed for the employer.

Qualifying LMIA-exempt categories for CRS arranged employment points include:

  • International agreements — work permits issued under bilateral or multilateral trade agreements. The largest of these is CUSMA (the Canada-United States-Mexico Agreement, formerly NAFTA), which covers US and Mexican citizens in specific professional categories. CUSMA does not cover Brazilian citizens, EU nationals, UK nationals, or any country other than the US and Mexico. This is one of the most common misconceptions among prospective candidates. Other agreements include CETA (covering EU professionals in narrow categories) and several bilateral agreements with smaller scope.
  • Intra-company transferees (ICT) — executives, senior managers, and specialized knowledge workers transferred from a foreign branch, subsidiary, or affiliate of a Canadian company. The candidate must have worked for the foreign entity for at least one continuous year in the three years before transfer.
  • Reciprocal employment including the International Experience Canada (IEC) program — young workers from countries with IEC agreements with Canada (Brazil has an IEC agreement; the categories include Working Holiday, Young Professionals, and International Co-op).
  • Significant benefit — work permits issued under section 205(a) of the Immigration and Refugee Protection Regulations where the officer is satisfied the work will create significant social, cultural, or economic benefit to Canada.

Categories that are LMIA-exempt but do not confer CRS points include open work permits (such as spousal open work permits, post-graduation work permits when used in non-qualifying roles, and bridging open work permits) and several other narrower exemptions.

The practical implication: a Brazilian on an IEC Working Holiday permit who is working full-time in a TEER 0, 1, 2, or 3 role for a Canadian employer can, after one year with that employer, potentially claim the 50 CRS points on a formal job offer. A Brazilian on a CUSMA professional permit cannot, because CUSMA doesn't cover Brazilians.

Key 2024-2025 changes: LMIA fraud crackdown and CRS impact

The 2024-2025 period saw the most significant changes to the LMIA/CRS framework since arranged employment points were introduced. The headline changes:

  • March 2025 — temporary removal of arranged employment CRS points. IRCC announced the removal of CRS points for arranged employment as part of the broader response to documented LMIA fraud (including investigations into LMIAs being sold for cash to candidates) and to reduce incentives for fraudulent job offers. The framework above describes how the points work when they are in effect; the current status needs to be verified on IRCC's website before any candidate builds a strategy around these points.
  • LMIA processing tightening. ESDC tightened review of low-wage and intermediate-wage LMIAs and limited the proportion of low-wage temporary foreign workers an employer can hire in certain regions.
  • Employer compliance enforcement. The non-compliant employer list grew, and ESDC increased site visits and post-issuance audits.
  • Recruitment requirements. Advertising and recruitment minimums were strengthened across most LMIA streams.

These changes are why the "is a job offer worth pursuing?" question is more nuanced in 2026 than it was in 2022. The mechanics of how points are awarded haven't fundamentally changed, but the political environment around LMIAs and their CRS treatment is more volatile.

How a spouse's job offer affects YOUR CRS

A common question: if my spouse gets a Canadian job offer, do I get CRS points for it?

The direct answer is no. Arranged employment CRS points are awarded only to the principal applicant on the Express Entry profile. Your spouse's job offer does not directly add CRS points to your score as the principal applicant.

Where a spouse's situation does affect the family equation is indirectly: if your spouse gets a qualifying work permit in Canada (including a closed employer-specific permit on the back of an LMIA), they may become eligible to be the principal applicant on a separate Express Entry profile. The family then chooses which spouse has the higher overall CRS as principal applicant.

Separately, the spouse open work permit (SOWP) attached to certain primary work permits affects family mobility but not the principal applicant's CRS score directly.

Strategic considerations: is it worth pursuing a job offer if your CRS is already 470+

The cost-benefit calculation depends on three things:

  1. How far below the cutoff are you? If you're at 480 in a year where general draws are at 540 and STEM draws are at 480, you're already competitive in the STEM category. Chasing 50 points through an LMIA process that takes six months and depends on an employer's willingness to spend $1,000 and navigate advertising requirements may be slower than just waiting for the next STEM draw.
  2. Is there a clear employer match? If you already have a job offer or active conversation with a Canadian employer in your field, the conversation about LMIA support is straightforward. If you're cold-pitching the LMIA process to employers as a hiring tool, the conversion rate is low; most Canadian employers will not run an LMIA for a candidate they haven't already chosen.
  3. What's the current state of arranged employment points? Given the March 2025 changes and ongoing policy review, building a strategy that depends entirely on those points carries policy risk. Job offers that lead to a closed work permit and Canadian work experience (which then builds CEC eligibility and Canadian work experience CRS points) remain valuable even if the arranged-employment-specific points are suspended.

For most candidates above 470 with strong language scores and at least three years of skilled work experience, the higher-leverage moves are: push language to CLB 10 in all four abilities, complete an ECA if you haven't (see your ECA pathway), and apply to PNP streams aligned with your profile. Chasing an LMIA-backed job offer is rarely the first move, even when arranged employment points are fully in effect.

Common pitfalls

  • NOC mismatch. The job offer is in a TEER 4 or TEER 5 occupation despite a senior-sounding title. Driver, line cook, retail supervisor, and similar roles often sit at TEER 4 or 5 and don't qualify for arranged employment points.
  • Part-time below the threshold. The offer is for 25 hours per week instead of 30. No partial credit.
  • Expired or invalid LMIA. The LMIA was issued for a temporary foreign worker stream that doesn't support PR, or the LMIA has already expired by the time the candidate is invited to apply.
  • Employer on the non-compliant list. ESDC publishes the list of employers found to have violated TFW Program conditions. An LMIA from an employer on that list is invalid.
  • Job offer letter missing the duration commitment. The offer must explicitly commit to at least one year of full-time work after PR is granted. A standard "at will" employment letter without the duration language won't satisfy the IRCC officer.
  • CUSMA confusion. Candidates assuming they can use CUSMA-based LMIA exemption when they aren't US or Mexican citizens.
  • Specialized knowledge ICT misclassification. ICT specialized knowledge is a narrow category; not every senior individual contributor qualifies. The bar is genuinely "advanced proprietary knowledge of the company's products, processes, or systems."

CTA

The arranged employment CRS lever can be powerful when it applies, but the framework is narrow, the underlying process is slow and employer-dependent, and the policy environment around LMIA-backed CRS points has been actively changing since 2024. For most candidates, the right question is not "how do I get 50 or 200 points from a job offer," it's "given my full profile, where does pursuing a job offer rank against other point-improvement moves I could make in the same timeframe?"

If you're not sure whether chasing an LMIA-backed offer is worth the time and employer-side cost for your specific profile, or whether your existing LMIA-exempt work permit actually qualifies for arranged employment points, a structured strategy session with an RCIC will give you a concrete answer rather than more guesswork.
