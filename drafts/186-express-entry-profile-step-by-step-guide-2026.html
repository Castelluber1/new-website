How to Create Your Express Entry Profile in 2026: Step-by-Step Guide

10 min read•2026-05-17

Creating your Express Entry profile is the moment your Canadian permanent residence plan stops being theory and becomes a live file in IRCC's system. The profile itself is free, takes most applicants between 60 and 120 minutes to complete, and stays active in the Express Entry pool for 12 months. But the time you actually spend inside the IRCC portal is the easy part. The decisions you make in each section, how you classify your work experience, how you list a spouse, which education credentials you attach, directly determine the Comprehensive Ranking System (CRS) score you receive, and therefore whether you ever get an Invitation to Apply (ITA).

This guide walks through the process exactly as you will see it on the IRCC Secure Account, step by step, and flags the choices that consistently sink otherwise-strong candidates. If you have not yet confirmed which Express Entry stream you qualify for , Federal Skilled Worker, Canadian Experience Class, or Federal Skilled Trades, start by reviewing the Express Entry overview page and the CRS scoring breakdown first, then come back here when you are ready to file.

Before You Open the IRCC Portal: Four Things You Must Have Ready

Do not start the profile until all four of the following are in hand. The portal will let you save partial progress, but missing documents are the single largest source of profile errors and post-ITA refusals.

1. A valid language test result. For English you need an IELTS General Training or CELPIP-General report. For French you need TEF Canada or TCF Canada. The result must be less than two years old on the day you submit the profile (and must still be valid on the day you submit your permanent residence application after ITA). If you want to claim points for both official languages, which can add up to 50 CRS points in the right combination, you need a valid test for each.

2. An Educational Credential Assessment (ECA), if your degree or diploma was earned outside Canada. The ECA report must come from an IRCC-designated organization (WES, IQAS, ICAS, ICES, CES, or a regulated professional body for medical and pharmacy credentials). ECAs are valid for five years from the date of issue. If you completed all your post-secondary education in Canada, you do not need an ECA, but you do need the original Canadian credential. For a head-to-head comparison of providers and turnaround times, see our ECA guide.

3. Your passport. The profile asks for your passport number, country of issue, and expiry date. Use the passport you intend to travel on. If your passport expires within the next 12 months, renew it before you submit. IRCC will not issue a Confirmation of Permanent Residence valid beyond your passport expiry.

4. Documentation of every period of skilled work experience you intend to claim. This means employment reference letters that list your job title, dates of employment, hours per week, salary, and a detailed description of duties. You will not upload these letters at the profile stage, but you need them in front of you so you can answer every question accurately. The duties listed in your letter must match the NOC 2021 code you select. If they do not, your application will be refused after ITA for misrepresentation of work experience, one of the most common refusal grounds we see at the firm.

Step 1: Create or Sign Into Your IRCC Secure Account

Go to canada.ca and select "Sign in or create an account." You have two sign-in methods: GCKey (a username and password issued by the Government of Canada) or a Sign-in Partner (your online banking credentials through select Canadian banks). Both methods land you in the same IRCC Secure Account dashboard. If you are outside Canada, GCKey is usually simpler because Sign-in Partner is limited to a specific list of Canadian financial institutions.

Once inside, choose "Apply to come to Canada" and then "Express Entry (including Federal Skilled Worker)." The system will generate a personal reference code, write it down. If your session times out, you can resume your draft using that code.

Screenshot moment: the IRCC dashboard shows your active applications, draft profiles, and any messages from the department. Bookmark this page; you will return to it dozens of times across the next 6 to 18 months.

Step 2: Personal Information. Use Your Passport Name Exactly

The first substantive section asks for your legal name. Type it exactly as it appears on your passport, full given name(s), full surname, in the same order, with the same spelling, including any accents or hyphens. Do not use the name you go by socially, the Anglicized version you use at work, or a maiden name unless that is what is printed on the passport.

This is the most underestimated step in the entire profile. A mismatch between your profile name and your passport at the post-ITA stage triggers manual processing, identity verification requests, and in some cases a refusal. Fix it here, on day one, and you remove a future risk for zero cost.

The same section asks for your country of birth, country of citizenship, country of current residence, and date of birth. All four must match your passport and any other ID you submit later.

Step 3: Marital Status. A Strategic Decision, Not Just a Form Field

IRCC recognizes the following statuses: single, married, common-law partner (cohabiting continuously for at least 12 months in a conjugal relationship), divorced, separated, widowed, and annulled marriage. Select the one that is legally true on the day you submit the profile.

If you are married or in a common-law relationship, you must then decide whether your spouse will be a principal applicant joining you to Canada or a non-accompanying spouse. This is where strategy enters.

When a spouse accompanies you, the system calculates a "with-spouse" CRS score that includes points for your spouse's language ability (up to 20 points), education (up to 10 points), and Canadian work experience (up to 10 points), but reduces the single-applicant points you receive for your own core human capital. In many couples the with-spouse total is higher; in others, particularly where the spouse has no language test or weaker credentials, the without-spouse total wins by a wide margin.

Run both scenarios in the IRCC CRS calculator before you commit. The decision you make on this screen is binding for the duration of the profile and cannot be quietly reversed at the ITA stage.

Step 4: Language Ability. Enter the Scores, Not the CLB

You will be asked for your test type, test date, registration number, and the four individual scores (listening, reading, writing, speaking). Enter the raw test scores. IRCC's system automatically converts them to Canadian Language Benchmark (CLB) levels and awards CRS points.

If you took a second test in the other official language, add it in the same section. Even a CLB 5 in French on top of a strong English result can be worth 25 CRS points, meaningful in any draw, decisive in some.

Double-check the test date and registration number against your physical test report card. A typo here will not be caught until post-ITA when IRCC requests the report, and at that point you cannot edit the profile.

Step 5: Education. List Every Credential With Its ECA Number

For each post-secondary credential, enter the level (bachelor's, master's, doctorate, etc.), the field of study, the country of study, the institution, the start and completion dates, and, for foreign credentials, the ECA report number, the issuing body, and the date of issue.

List every credential you have, even if only your highest will be used to award points. If you have two bachelor's degrees plus a one-year postgraduate diploma, list all three. The "two or more credentials, one of which is three years or longer" combination is worth more CRS points than a single bachelor's alone, and IRCC will not infer this for you, the credentials must be in the profile.

If your ECA is more than five years old on the date you intend to submit the profile, you need a new one. There is no extension.

Step 6: Work Experience. NOC 2021 Is the Hinge

For every period of paid, skilled work experience in the last ten years, you enter the job title, employer name, country, start and end dates, hours per week, and, critically, the NOC 2021 code.

NOC 2021 is the current National Occupational Classification. The older NOC 2016 four-digit codes are no longer accepted in the Express Entry portal. NOC 2021 uses a five-digit TEER (Training, Education, Experience and Responsibilities) structure: the second digit is the TEER category (0 through 5), and only TEER 0, 1, 2, and 3 occupations qualify for Express Entry purposes.

Go to the NOC 2021 search tool, find your occupation, and read the full description, including the "main duties" list. At least 80% of those main duties must match what you actually did, and you must have performed the lead statement of the occupation. If the match is partial, look for a closer code. For a deeper walk-through of TEER categorization and how to choose between adjacent codes, see our TEER guide.

Hours matter as much as duties. IRCC counts work experience by hours, not months. One year of full-time experience equals 1,560 hours (30 hours per week × 52 weeks). Part-time and multiple part-time roles can be combined to reach the 1,560-hour threshold, but you must have worked at least 15 hours per week in any role you count, and the work must have been paid (volunteer work, internships without pay, and self-directed study do not qualify).

Miscounting hours is the second most common post-ITA refusal we see. Pull your tax records, employment letters, and pay stubs and do the math twice.

Step 7: Job Offer. Only If You Actually Have One

A "job offer" in Express Entry has a narrow legal definition: it is an offer of continuous, full-time, paid, non-seasonal employment of at least one year, in a TEER 0, 1, 2, or 3 occupation, supported by either a positive Labour Market Impact Assessment (LMIA) or an LMIA-exempt work permit issued under specific categories.

If you have one, enter the LMIA number (the nine-digit reference starting with "A") or the LMIA-exempt code and the IMM number of your work permit. As of the spring 2025 policy change, additional CRS points for arranged employment were temporarily removed from the Express Entry system for most occupations; check the IRCC web page for the current state of the arranged-employment point allocation before relying on it for your CRS projection.

If you do not have a qualifying job offer, leave this section as "No." A casual offer letter from a Canadian employer that has not gone through LMIA or an LMIA-exempt category does not count and should not be entered here.

Step 8: Provincial Nomination. Enter the Certificate Number

If you have received a provincial nomination certificate from a Provincial Nominee Program stream aligned with Express Entry, enter the nomination certificate number and the issuing province. The 600 additional CRS points from a provincial nomination effectively guarantee an ITA in the next applicable draw.

If you have applied to a PNP but have not yet received the nomination certificate, do not enter anything here. You can update the profile when the certificate arrives, the profile remains editable until you submit a post-ITA application.

Step 9: Review Your CRS Score Before You Submit

After completing every section, the portal calculates and displays your CRS score on the review page. Read it carefully against your own pre-submission calculation. A 20- to 50-point gap between expectation and reality almost always traces back to one of three places: a spouse you forgot to mark as accompanying, an education credential you listed at the wrong level, or work experience you entered in months when it should have been counted in hours-to-years.

If the score is far off, do not submit. Go back, find the discrepancy, and fix it. A profile with the wrong CRS will sit in the pool unread; the time you spend correcting it now is recovered many times over.

Step 10: Submit and Wait for the AOR

When you click submit, the system issues an electronic Acknowledgment of Receipt almost immediately. The AOR includes your profile number, your CRS score on the date of submission, and your expiry date (12 months from submission). Print it, save the PDF, and keep both somewhere you will not lose them.

Your profile now sits in the Express Entry pool. IRCC holds general, category-based, and program-specific draws roughly every two weeks. If your CRS meets or exceeds the cutoff in a draw for which you are eligible, you receive an Invitation to Apply through your IRCC account.

After You Submit: Be ITA-Ready Inside 60 Days

When the ITA arrives, you have 60 calendar days to submit a complete permanent residence application with every supporting document uploaded, every form completed, and the government processing fees paid. Sixty days sounds like a lot until you are chasing reference letters from three former employers, a fresh police certificate from a country with a 90-day issuance window, and an upgraded medical exam.

Do not wait for the ITA to start gathering documents. The moment your profile is submitted, begin pulling:

  • Employment reference letters on company letterhead for every NOC-coded role
  • Police certificates from every country you have lived in for 6+ months since age 18
  • Updated proof of funds (six months of bank statements, balance must meet the published threshold for your family size on the day you submit)
  • An upfront medical exam from a panel physician (optional but recommended; saves the 30-day medical request window)
  • Digital photos meeting IRCC photo specifications
  • A reference letter from your current employer if you are still working

Common Mistakes That Cost Candidates Their ITA

  1. NOC code that does not match the actual duties. Selecting a higher-TEER code to boost CRS, then being unable to produce a reference letter that demonstrates the lead duties of that NOC. Result: refusal for misrepresentation.

  2. Name spelled differently than passport. Causes manual review at every subsequent step. Almost always preventable.

  3. Work experience hours undercounted or overcounted. Part-time roles forgotten, unpaid leave counted, or a 35-hour-per-week role rounded up to 40. IRCC verifies against pay records.

  4. Expired ECA. A five-year ECA that lapses one week before submission is invalid. Order the new one before the old one expires.

  5. Spouse listed as non-accompanying when they intend to come. Cannot be reversed at ITA without restarting the profile. Make the strategic decision before you click submit.

  6. Language test more than two years old at submission. Common with candidates who took the test early "to be prepared" and then took six months to gather other documents.
