Canada Visitor Visa Processing Time for Argentinians in 2026

7 min read•2026-05-20

If you are an Argentine citizen planning a trip to Canada in 2026, IRCC's current processing time for visitor visa applications from Argentina is 16 days. That figure comes directly from the official IRCC processing times tool, last checked May 2026. Sixteen days is among the shorter processing windows in Latin America, but biometrics and proper preparation still determine how your total timeline shapes up.

Argentina has lower visitor visa application volumes to Canada compared to countries like Brazil or Mexico. That lower volume is a significant reason why Argentine applicants see faster processing times. Still, Canada does not offer visa-free entry to Argentine passport holders, and a Temporary Resident Visa (TRV) is required for any visit, whether for tourism, family, business, or events. A faster review window is an advantage, but the quality of your application still determines the outcome.

How to Check the Current Processing Time

Processing times are country-specific and IRCC updates them on a rolling basis. The 16-day figure above reflects data from May 2026. By the time you are reading this, that number may have changed. The official IRCC tool is the only reliable source.

Here are the exact steps:

  1. Go to the IRCC processing times tool.
  2. Under "What type of application is it?", select Temporary residence (visiting, studying, working).
  3. Under "What application are you checking?", select Visitor visa (from outside Canada).
  4. Under "What country are you applying from?", select Argentina.
  5. Click "Get processing time" to see the current estimate.

What "16 Days" Actually Means

The published processing time is not a deadline and it is not a promise of approval. IRCC calculates it based on how long it took to process 80% of complete applications from Argentina during a recent reference period. One in five Argentine applicants will wait longer than 16 days. For those whose files require additional scrutiny, the wait can be considerably longer.

Three things determine which side of that average your application ends up on.

Completeness at submission. The 16-day clock applies only to applications that arrive complete. A missing document, an inconsistency between forms, or anything that prompts IRCC to send a document request will pause your file. The time you spend gathering and uploading those documents does not appear in the published figure, but it adds directly to your real timeline.

The figure is Argentina-specific. Argentina's relatively lower application volume compared to larger Latin American countries contributes to the 16-day estimate. Do not apply numbers you see quoted for other countries to your application. The tool gives you a country-specific figure for a reason.

Financial documentation context. Argentine applicants face particular attention on financial documentation due to Argentina's well-known currency volatility and economic conditions. Officers want to see financial stability, and what counts as clear documentation may require more context than it would for applicants from countries with more stable currencies. A file that addresses this clearly moves through more smoothly than one that leaves the officer to interpret irregular financial records without context.

Think of 16 days as the baseline for a well-prepared, complete Argentine application. It is the minimum to plan around, not the target.

Biometrics: Still Required, Still Adds Time

A faster IRCC review window does not mean biometrics are skipped. Most Argentine applicants applying for a Canadian visitor visa for the first time, or for the first time in more than 10 years, are required to provide biometrics. This step happens after you submit your online application and it adds time to your total timeline regardless of how quickly IRCC reviews the visa itself.

After submitting your application online, you will receive a Biometric Instruction Letter (BIL) from IRCC. You then have 30 days to attend an appointment at a Visa Application Centre (VAC) in Argentina to provide your fingerprints and photograph. The VAC serving Argentine applicants is located in Buenos Aires.

Appointment availability at the Buenos Aires VAC fluctuates with demand. Book your appointment as soon as your biometrics instruction letter arrives, not after you have read it a few times. Slots during busy periods fill up faster than most applicants expect.

In practical terms, even with the shorter 16-day IRCC review, your total timeline from application submission to a final decision is closer to 5 to 7 weeks when biometrics are factored in. The biometrics step is what makes the difference between the IRCC published time and your actual wait.

If you provided biometrics to Canada within the last 10 years, you will not need to go through the process again. Check the date on your previous Canadian application to confirm your biometrics status.

Building Your Trip Timeline

If you are planning a trip to Canada from Argentina, here is a practical framework for building your application timeline.

Add together: time to book and attend a biometrics appointment in Buenos Aires (up to 2 weeks in some periods), a few days for biometrics to process after your appointment, 16 days for IRCC visa review once biometrics are received, and a buffer of 1 to 2 weeks for any document requests or unexpected delays. The realistic total is 7 to 8 weeks from application submission to a decision, and potentially a bit shorter given the faster IRCC review window.

Working backwards from your planned travel date, submit your application at least 8 weeks in advance. If you are travelling for a specific event with a fixed date, build in extra time. Even with Argentina's faster processing times, a refusal or document request resets the clock.

Do not book non-refundable travel until your visa is in hand. A 16-day estimate is an average, and an application under review is not a confirmed trip.

What Officers Look for in Argentine Visitor Visa Applications

Argentine applicants benefit from lower application volumes and a generally strong track record with Canadian immigration. But officers still apply the same core criteria, and there are specific areas where Argentine files receive close attention.

Ties to Argentina. As with all visitor visa applications, the central question is whether you will leave Canada at the end of your authorized stay. Officers look for genuine, documented ties to Argentina: stable employment, a business, property, family members who depend on you and who are not in Canada, and ongoing obligations that require your presence at home. The ties argument should be specific and supported by documents, not stated as a general intention.

Financial documentation that accounts for currency context. Argentina's economic situation, including currency controls and inflation, means that financial documents from Argentine applicants sometimes require additional context. Officers want to see a stable financial situation and clear evidence that you can support yourself in Canada. If your financial records involve foreign currency accounts, foreign savings, or income structures that are specific to the Argentine economic context, include a brief explanation. Clarity here reduces the chance of delays or requests for more information.

Purpose of visit clearly established. Whether you are visiting family, touring national parks, attending a business conference, or joining a special event, document your purpose specifically. Officers reviewing Argentine files expect clear itineraries, invitation letters where relevant, and documentation that ties together the stated purpose of visit with your overall profile.

Travel history to Europe or the United States as supporting context. Argentine applicants with a documented history of travel to Europe or the United States, with timely departures, can reference that record. A history of international travel with proper compliance supports the argument that you travel within authorized conditions. It adds context to your file, though it does not replace a strong ties argument.

Disclosure of prior refusals. Canadian immigration forms ask directly about prior visa refusals from any country. Omitting this information is treated as misrepresentation, which carries consequences far more serious than the original refusal itself. Disclose all prior refusals accurately and explain what has changed since then.
