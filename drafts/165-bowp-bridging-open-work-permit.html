Bridging Open Work Permit (BOWP): How to Keep Working While Waiting for Your PR in 2026

10 min read•2026-05-16

You filed your Permanent Residence application months ago. Your current work permit expires in 90 days. IRCC is still processing. Your employer is asking what happens next, and you are wondering whether you will have to stop working, leave the country, or scramble for a new LMIA.

This is exactly the scenario the Bridging Open Work Permit (BOWP) was designed to solve. It is one of the most underused tools in Canadian immigration, and the rules around it have quietly become more generous since 2024. If you are in the final stretch of an Express Entry, PNP, or Quebec PR application and your permit is running out, the BOWP can buy you up to 24 more months of legal work authorization with any employer in Canada.

This guide walks through how the BOWP works in 2026, who actually qualifies, when to file, the documents you need, and the mistakes that quietly cost people their status.

What the BOWP Is and Why It Exists

The Bridging Open Work Permit is an open work permit issued by IRCC to people who already have a Permanent Residence application in progress under specific economic immigration programs. The purpose is in the name: it bridges the gap between an expiring temporary work permit and a final PR decision.

Before the BOWP existed, applicants in the queue for PR often had to apply for an entirely new closed work permit, find a new LMIA, or risk losing status while IRCC processed their file. The federal government created the BOWP to keep skilled foreign workers in Canada and in the labour market during that waiting period, instead of forcing them to interrupt their careers or leave.

The key word is "open." A BOWP is not tied to a single employer or a single job. It works like a Post-Graduation Work Permit in that sense: you can work for almost any employer, in almost any role, anywhere in Canada, for the duration of the permit. There are narrow exceptions (employers on the IRCC ineligible list, jobs that require additional medicals you have not done, etc.), but the freedom it provides is the entire point of the program.

Who Qualifies in 2026

To be eligible for a BOWP, you must have a PR application in progress under one of the following economic classes:

  • Federal Skilled Worker Program (FSWP)
  • Canadian Experience Class (CEC)
  • Federal Skilled Trades Program (FSTP)
  • Provincial Nominee Program (PNP), once you have submitted the federal PR application (not just the provincial nomination)
  • Atlantic Immigration Program (AIP)
  • Quebec Skilled Worker Program (QSW), Quebec Experience Program (PEQ), or Quebec business programs, where applicable
  • Caregiver pilots and the Home Child Care Provider / Home Support Worker pathways
  • Rural and Francophone Community Immigration pilots (newly added classes)

If your PR file is in any of these streams and IRCC has formally acknowledged receipt of it, you are in the eligible universe. If you are still waiting on a provincial nomination, or if you have not yet submitted your federal PR application, you are not yet eligible. The clock for BOWP eligibility starts at the federal AOR, not at the nomination.

The Three Hard Requirements

Beyond being in an eligible PR class, IRCC will check three specific conditions before issuing a BOWP. Get any of these wrong and the application is refused.

1. You must be physically in Canada when you apply. The BOWP is an inside-Canada extension. If you are abroad when your current permit expires, you cannot apply for a BOWP from outside. You would instead need a different work permit category or a visitor entry first.

2. You must currently hold a valid work permit, or be on maintained status from a work permit. This is the most common trip-up. If your work permit has already expired and you did not file an extension before the expiry date, you have lost status. A BOWP cannot fix lost status by itself. You would need to restore status first, which is a separate process with its own 90-day window and risks.

3. You must have received Acknowledgment of Receipt (AOR) for your PR application. This is the email from IRCC confirming your PR file has been accepted into processing. Applying for a BOWP before AOR is the single most common refusal reason. The system has no way to verify your PR file exists yet, so the officer has nothing to bridge to.

When to Apply: The 4-Month Window

The unofficial best-practice window is to apply for your BOWP within about 4 months of your current work permit's expiry date. There is no hard rule that says "exactly 120 days," but in practice applying earlier than that risks a refusal on the grounds that there is no immediate bridging need, and applying later risks running out of status while you wait for a decision.

The ideal sequence looks like this:

  1. Submit your PR application.
  2. Wait for AOR (anywhere from a few days for Express Entry to several weeks for PNP/Quebec).
  3. Once AOR is in hand AND you are within ~4 months of your permit expiry, file the BOWP.
  4. Continue working under your existing permit. Once it expires, maintained status kicks in automatically until IRCC decides the BOWP.

If your AOR arrives more than 4 months before your permit expires, simply wait. There is no benefit to filing earlier, and several risks.

BOWP Validity: Up to 24 Months

Historically the BOWP was issued for 12 months. In 2023 IRCC extended the standard validity to 24 months to reflect the longer real-world PR processing times, and that 24-month standard has held into 2026.

In practice the permit is issued for 24 months OR until your passport expires, whichever comes first. This is why making sure your passport has at least 2 years of validity left before you apply is one of the easiest wins available. If your passport expires in 14 months, your BOWP will too, regardless of how generous IRCC is feeling that day.

The "Open" Advantage

A closed work permit ties you to one employer, often one job title, often one work location. If that employer lays you off, your permit becomes effectively useless. You either find a new LMIA-backed job (slow, expensive, uncertain) or you lose status.

The BOWP removes all of that. While it is active you can:

  • Switch employers without any new paperwork to IRCC
  • Take on a second job
  • Change industries entirely
  • Negotiate a raise without your employer holding your status over you
  • Relocate to a different province

The only meaningful restrictions are on regulated occupations (you still need provincial licensing if your job requires it) and on employers flagged as non-compliant.

Documents You Will Need

A typical BOWP application includes:

  • IMM 5710 (Application to Change Conditions or Extend Stay as a Worker)
  • Copy of your current work permit
  • Copy of your passport biographical page
  • Proof of AOR for your PR application (the IRCC letter or Express Entry portal screenshot)
  • Digital photo meeting IRCC specs
  • Processing fee plus open work permit holder fee
  • Updated employment letter (helpful but not always mandatory)
  • Medical exam results if your last medical is more than 12 months old

For PNP applicants specifically, include both the provincial nomination letter and the federal AOR. Officers occasionally refuse files where only the nomination is provided, on the theory that the federal PR application has not technically begun.

Common Mistakes That Quietly Wreck Files

Applying before AOR. The number one refusal reason. The PR file has to exist in IRCC's system first.

Letting status lapse. If your current permit expires and you have not filed any extension, you are out of status the moment the clock strikes midnight. Maintained status only applies if you filed the extension while the permit was still valid.

Filing for a closed work permit "just in case." Once IRCC issues you a new closed permit, it generally overwrites your maintained status protections. People sometimes file an LMIA-based permit as a backup, and accidentally lock themselves into a worse position than just waiting for the BOWP.

Changing NOC into something not aligned with your PR file. This is a softer issue. The BOWP itself does not restrict your NOC, but if your underlying PR application was approved on the basis of CEC work in a TEER 1 role, taking a TEER 4 job during the BOWP period can complicate the final PR decision, especially if IRCC requests updated employment letters.

Forgetting biometrics. If you have not given biometrics in the last 10 years, you will need to. Missing the biometrics step delays the BOWP by weeks.

BOWP vs Implied (Maintained) Status

These two get confused constantly. They are not the same thing.

Maintained status (formerly called implied status) is automatic. If you file ANY work, study, or visitor permit extension before your current status expires, you continue under the same conditions until IRCC decides the new application. You do not get a new document. There is nothing in your hand to show an employer.

A BOWP is an actual permit document, issued after IRCC reviews your application. It gives you new, broader conditions (open instead of closed) and a new expiry date up to 24 months out.

In a typical BOWP timeline you use BOTH: you file the BOWP, your old permit expires, you continue working under maintained status with the same closed-employer conditions until IRCC approves the BOWP, then your conditions become open the day the BOWP is issued.

Next Step

If you have submitted a PR application in any of the eligible classes and your current work permit is heading toward expiry, the BOWP is almost certainly the cleanest path forward. The difficulty is not the application itself; it is the timing and the eligibility verification. One wrong assumption about AOR, NOC, or expiry dates can end with weeks of unpaid leave and a refused file.
