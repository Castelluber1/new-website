Canada Work Permit Processing Time for Brazilians in 2026

7 min read•2026-05-20

For Brazilian nationals applying for a Canadian work permit from outside Canada, the current IRCC processing time is 5 weeks. That figure is specific to Brazil, pulled directly from the IRCC processing times tool on May 13, 2026. It is a useful number, but it only tells part of the story. This article explains what the 5 weeks actually covers, what it does not cover, and how the path to a work permit looks different depending on which stream you are applying through.

How to check your processing time on the IRCC tool

IRCC publishes a publicly accessible tool where you can look up current processing times by application type and country of residence. The result is specific to where you live now, not where you are from. If you are a Brazilian citizen living in Brazil, run it with Brazil selected. If you have relocated to another country, use that country instead.

Steps to check:

  1. Go to the IRCC processing times tool
  2. Select "Temporary residence (visiting, studying, working)"
  3. Select "Work permit (from outside Canada)"
  4. Select "Brazil"
  5. Click "Get processing time" — currently 5 weeks

The tool is updated weekly. The figure can shift up or down as IRCC inventory changes, so check it again close to your intended submission date rather than relying on a number you read weeks earlier.

What "5 weeks" actually means

The number published by IRCC represents the time it took to finalize 80% of applications in that category over a recent historical window. It is not an average, not a median, and not a promise. One in five applicants will wait longer, sometimes considerably longer, and that is within the normal range of the published standard.

The clock starts only when IRCC considers your application complete, meaning:

  • All required forms are uploaded and correctly filled out
  • Government fees are paid
  • Supporting documents are present and consistent with each other
  • Biometrics have been submitted, if required

A missing document, a blurry passport scan, or a mismatch between your job offer letter and the LMIA means your application is not yet in the queue. The 5-week clock has not started.

Equally important: this figure covers only the IRCC work permit processing stage. It does not include any employer-side preparation that must happen before you can apply. That distinction is critical for most Brazilian work permit applicants, as explained below.

The two-track reality for Brazilian workers

Brazilian nationals applying for a Canadian work permit from abroad typically fall into one of two tracks. The 5-week IRCC figure means something different depending on which one applies to you.

Track 1: LMIA-backed work permits

If your Canadian employer needs to go through the Temporary Foreign Worker Program (TFWP), they first need a Labour Market Impact Assessment (LMIA) from Employment and Social Development Canada (ESDC). The LMIA confirms that no qualified Canadian or permanent resident was available for the role. You cannot submit your work permit application to IRCC until the LMIA is approved.

Current LMIA processing by ESDC adds its own timeline before the IRCC stage even begins:

  • High-wage stream: typically 3 to 9 weeks depending on occupation and current ESDC volumes
  • Low-wage stream: similar range, with additional scrutiny in certain sectors
  • Global Talent Stream (specific tech occupations): 2-week service standard from ESDC when conditions are met

The full picture for an LMIA-backed work permit looks like this: your employer prepares and submits the LMIA application, waits for ESDC approval (weeks to months), then you submit your work permit application to IRCC, and IRCC processes it in approximately 5 weeks. The 5-week figure is the final step, not the total race.

Track 2: LMIA-exempt work permits

For Brazilians applying under an LMIA-exempt category, the picture is different. No LMIA is required, and the employer-side step is faster. Common LMIA-exempt categories for Brazilian nationals include:

  • International Experience Canada (IEC), Working Holiday: Brazil is an IEC-eligible country. Working Holiday participants apply directly under this open work permit stream. There is no employer requirement before applying.
  • CUSMA (formerly NAFTA) professionals and intra-company transferees: Applicable when the job qualifies under a trade agreement or intra-company transfer exemption.
  • Spousal open work permit: Spouses and common-law partners of eligible Canadian workers or international students may apply for an open work permit from outside Canada. The 5-week processing time applies, but eligibility depends on the principal applicant's permit category and status.
  • Other International Mobility Program (IMP) categories: Including significant benefit, charitable, and religious worker exemptions.

For LMIA-exempt permits, the employer typically submits an Offer of Employment through the IRCC Employer Portal before you apply. That step takes a few days rather than weeks. The IRCC 5-week processing time is much closer to your actual total timeline.

Brazilian nationals and IEC Working Holiday

If you are a Brazilian between 18 and 35 years old and want to work in Canada without a specific job offer, the IEC Working Holiday stream is the most direct route. Brazil is a participating country, and the Working Holiday permit is an open work permit, meaning you can work for any employer, in any province, in almost any occupation.

The IEC process involves submitting a profile, receiving an invitation to apply, and then applying for the open work permit. Processing for IEC participants falls under the IRCC work permit processing time framework. For a detailed breakdown of the IEC timeline and application process, see our article on IEC Working Holiday for Brazilians.

Biometrics: add 30 days if this is your first Canadian application

If you have never provided biometrics for a Canadian immigration application, or if your biometrics on file are more than 10 years old, you will receive a biometrics instruction letter (BIL) after submitting your work permit application. You have 30 days from that letter to attend a Visa Application Centre (VAC) and provide your fingerprints and photo. IRCC will not continue processing until biometrics are received.

For Brazilian applicants, VACs operate in the following cities:

  • São Paulo
  • Rio de Janeiro
  • Brasília
  • Recife
  • Porto Alegre

If you are based outside those cities, factor in travel time and VAC appointment availability. In practice, biometrics add approximately 30 days to the total timeline for first-time applicants. Build this into your planning if your employer is counting on a specific start date.

Common delay causes for Brazilian applicants

Being in the 20% of applicants who wait longer than 5 weeks is not random. Most delays are tied to specific, preventable issues.

Incomplete LMIA package. Even after the LMIA is approved, the job offer letter you include with your work permit application must accurately reflect it. Mismatches between the LMIA job title, NOC code, wage, hours, or work location and what appears in your offer letter can trigger officer requests for additional information or a refusal.

Missing or inconsistent job offer letter details. A letter that omits the NOC code, lists the wrong wage, does not specify the work location, or lacks the LMIA number (when required) will create problems. Officers check these details carefully, and a letter that does not align with the approved LMIA will stall your file.

Quebec Certificate of Acceptance (CAQ) required if working in Quebec. If your job is in Quebec, you need both a provincial CAQ from the Ministère de l'Immigration, de la Francisation et de l'Intégration (MIFI) and a federal work permit. These are two separate applications processed by two separate governments. You cannot submit the federal work permit application until the CAQ is issued. Quebec CAQ processing adds its own timeline before the IRCC clock even starts.

Police certificate not included if required. Some work permit categories and employer compliance reviews prompt additional background documentation. A Brazilian police certificate (antecedentes criminais) from the Polícia Federal is required in certain situations. If yours is needed and missing, your application will stall. The Brazilian federal police certificate takes several business days under normal circumstances, so request it early if there is any chance it will be required.

Medical examination for certain occupations. Work permit applicants whose jobs involve working with children or vulnerable populations, or in certain healthcare roles, may be required to complete an immigration medical exam (IME) before the permit is issued. If your occupation triggers this requirement and you have not completed the medical, IRCC will request it mid-process. Completing the medical proactively, before submitting, eliminates this delay entirely.

A Canadian work permit application from Brazil involves employer-side preparation, documentation that has to hold up to officer review, and timing decisions that affect how long the whole process takes.
