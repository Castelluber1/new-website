British Columbia for Newcomers: Why 67% of BC Immigrants Stay (and What to Know in 2026)

10 min read•2026-05-16

British Columbia keeps roughly two out of every three immigrants who land there. That 67% retention rate is one of the highest in Canada outside Ontario, and it tells you something important: most people who choose BC are not just passing through on the way to somewhere cheaper. They settle, raise families, switch jobs, buy homes (eventually), and stay. This guide covers what newcomers actually need to know before moving to BC in 2026, geography, the cost of living reality, immigration pathways, the job market, schools, healthcare, climate, and the Brazilian community that has quietly built itself across the Lower Mainland and the Fraser Valley.

A quick map of BC: four regions, four lifestyles

BC is the third-largest province by population (~5.6 million) and the third-largest by area. Most newcomers think "Vancouver" when they hear British Columbia, but the province is far more varied than that single skyline suggests.

Metro Vancouver. Roughly 2.6 million people across Vancouver, Burnaby, Surrey, Richmond, Coquitlam, and the rest of the Lower Mainland. This is where most newcomers land, where most jobs sit, and where housing costs are punishing. Surrey alone has the largest South Asian community in Canada; Richmond is majority Chinese-Canadian; Burnaby and the Tri-Cities have growing Brazilian, Korean, and Iranian populations.

The Fraser Valley. East of Metro Vancouver: Abbotsford, Chilliwack, Mission, Langley. Cheaper than Vancouver, still inside the same weather system, with farmland, mountains, and a growing population of newcomers priced out of the city. Abbotsford in particular has become a quiet hub for Brazilian families, it is where Up Immigration is based.

Vancouver Island. Victoria (the provincial capital), Nanaimo, the Comox Valley. Slower pace, ferry-dependent for mainland access, milder winters than anywhere else in Canada. Victoria is government, tech, tourism, and the University of Victoria.

The Okanagan and Interior. Kelowna, Vernon, Penticton, Kamloops. Dry continental climate, real summers (35°C+) and real winters with snow. Wine country, agriculture, and a growing tech scene in Kelowna. More affordable than the coast, with a different lifestyle.

The North. Prince George, Terrace, Prince Rupert, Fort St. John. Resource-based economies (forestry, mining, oil and gas), serious winters, smaller populations. The BC PNP runs a Northern stream with lower thresholds specifically because the region needs workers.

Cost of living: the BC reality check

Let's be direct. Vancouver is consistently ranked one of the most expensive cities in North America, and that single fact dictates much of what newcomer life looks like in BC.

Verify these against current CREA reports and your destination city's rental market. BC numbers shift fast.

The honest version: a $120,000 household income in Vancouver supports a two-bedroom rental, public transit, modest savings, and tight grocery budgets. The same income in Abbotsford supports a townhouse mortgage, a car, kids' activities, and meaningful savings. In Kelowna or Nanaimo, it supports comparable lifestyle to Abbotsford with different weather.

Newcomers who plan well usually land in the Fraser Valley, Vancouver Island, or the Okanagan rather than central Vancouver, then move closer to the city once income stabilizes. The reverse, landing in Vancouver and trying to outgrow it, leads to burnout faster than people expect.

BC PNP streams: the main pathway to PR

The BC Provincial Nominee Program (BC PNP) runs the most active provincial nomination program in Western Canada. There are two main categories with multiple sub-streams underneath.

Skills Immigration (the main category)

  • Skilled Worker. TEER 0, 1, 2, or 3 occupation with a BC job offer
  • Healthcare Professional, direct stream for RNs, LPNs, physicians, allied health
  • International Post-Graduate. Master's or PhD graduates from a BC institution, no job offer required for eligible fields
  • International Graduate, post-secondary BC graduates with a BC job offer in TEER 0-3
  • Entry Level and Semi-Skilled (ELSS). TEER 4/5 occupations specifically in tourism/hospitality, long-haul trucking, or food processing, in designated regions

Entrepreneur Immigration

  • Base category, for established business owners willing to invest in BC
  • Regional Pilot, for entrepreneurs willing to start businesses in smaller communities

The BC PNP also runs targeted draws for the Tech occupations list (currently around 35 in-demand tech NOCs), which has historically had lower CRS thresholds and weekly draws. If you are a software engineer, data professional, or product manager with a BC job offer, this is often the fastest route to PR in Western Canada.

For most newcomers the practical sequence is: arrive on a work permit or study permit, build BC work experience and a job offer, then apply through BC PNP Skilled Worker or Tech. The Provincial Nominee Program guide walks through the federal-provincial mechanics in more detail.

Top sectors and where the jobs are

BC's economy is more diversified than people give it credit for. The major sectors:

  • Technology. Vancouver is North America's third-largest film/animation/VFX hub, plus a strong cluster in gaming, AI, cloud, and SaaS. Major employers include Amazon, Microsoft, EA, Sony Imageworks, Hootsuite, Lululemon Digital, and a long tail of startups. Salaries trail Toronto by ~10-15% but housing-adjusted purchasing power often favours Toronto.
  • Healthcare. Chronic shortages across the province, especially nursing, family medicine, and allied health. Both the BC PNP Healthcare stream and federal Express Entry healthcare draws prioritize these professions.
  • Construction and skilled trades. Massive infrastructure pipeline (SkyTrain extensions, hospital builds, housing), strong demand for electricians, plumbers, carpenters, and project managers.
  • Tourism and hospitality. Year-round in Vancouver and Victoria; seasonal in Whistler, the Okanagan, and Tofino.
  • Natural resources. Forestry, mining, fishing, and clean energy, concentrated in the North and Interior.
  • Film and television. Direct and indirect employer of tens of thousands. "Hollywood North" is not marketing, it is structural.
  • Education. UBC, SFU, UVic, BCIT, and a network of colleges employ thousands of academics and administrative staff.

Salary reality check: comparable roles in Vancouver typically pay 10-20% less than Toronto and 15-25% less than Calgary in energy-adjacent fields. The compensation is the lifestyle, the weather, and the geography, not the paycheck.

Climate: the easiest Canadian winter

For Brazilians and other newcomers from warm climates, BC's coast is the gentlest possible introduction to Canada.

The Coast (Vancouver, Victoria, Fraser Valley). Winter temperatures hover around 3-7°C. Snow is rare and usually gone within days. Rain, on the other hand, is the defining feature: November through February can deliver 150+ mm per month. Summer is mild, dry, and beautiful, typically 22-28°C with low humidity.

The Okanagan and Interior. Real continental climate. Summers reach 35°C+ with low humidity (wine country exists for a reason). Winters bring real snow and temperatures down to -15°C in cold snaps.

The North. Serious winters. -25°C is normal in Prince George in January. Worth the trade only if the job, the community, or the pathway justifies it.

Compared to Toronto's -20°C polar vortex weeks or Winnipeg's -40°C reality, coastal BC is the soft landing of Canadian climates. The trade is the rain. Newcomers who hate grey wet winters move to the Okanagan or Vancouver Island; newcomers who hate cold stay on the coast and buy good rain jackets.

Housing: rent first, buy later

Almost every newcomer benefits from renting for the first 12-24 months. Reasons:

  1. You need BC credit history before any meaningful mortgage approval.
  2. You need to learn the neighbourhoods before committing $1M+.
  3. You will probably change cities within BC at least once.
  4. Down payment requirements (5-20% on $1M+) are brutal without local equity.

For rentals, expect to provide: BC employment letter, recent paystubs, references, and increasingly a credit check. New arrivals without Canadian credit often need to offer 6 months of rent upfront or find a co-signer. Brazilian-owned property managers in Surrey, Burnaby, and Abbotsford are sometimes more flexible with newcomer documentation.

For buyers eventually: BC has the federal foreign buyer ban (in effect through 2027) and a 20% Property Transfer Tax for non-residents. Permanent residents are exempt from both. First-time homebuyers (PR or citizen) can use the federal Home Buyers' Plan to draw on RRSPs tax-free for a down payment.

Schools and universities

K-12 education in BC is free for residents (including those on work permits in most cases, verify with the district). The system is well-regarded, with strong performance on international comparisons. Major school districts include Vancouver, Surrey (the largest in BC), Burnaby, Coquitlam, Richmond, and Abbotsford.

For post-secondary, BC has three flagship research universities:

  • UBC (University of British Columbia). Vancouver and Okanagan campuses, top-ranked globally, broad program offering. PGWP-eligible.
  • SFU (Simon Fraser University). Burnaby, Surrey, and downtown Vancouver campuses. Strong in business, computing, and communications. PGWP-eligible.
  • UVic (University of Victoria). Victoria. Particularly strong in law, ocean sciences, and humanities. PGWP-eligible.

Plus a strong network of polytechnics and colleges: BCIT (BC Institute of Technology), Capilano, Langara, Douglas, KPU, and the University of the Fraser Valley (Abbotsford). All offer PGWP-eligible programs at substantially lower tuition than the research universities.

International tuition for undergraduate programs typically runs $30,000-$45,000 per year at UBC/SFU/UVic, $18,000-$28,000 at polytechnics and colleges.

MSP healthcare: what to know

BC's Medical Services Plan (MSP) is the provincial public health insurance. The essentials:

  • No premium. BC eliminated MSP premiums in 2020. Coverage is funded through general taxation and the Employer Health Tax.
  • 3-month waiting period. Coverage begins the first day of the third month after you establish residency. A newcomer arriving February 15 is covered May 1.
  • What you need during the gap. Private travel health insurance, ideally arranged before arrival. Premiums run $50-150/month per adult depending on age and coverage.
  • What MSP covers. Medically necessary doctor visits, hospital stays, surgeries, diagnostic imaging, lab work. It does not cover dental, vision, prescription drugs (outside hospital), mental health beyond psychiatry, or paramedical (physio, massage, chiro). Most employers offer extended benefits to cover these.

Reality check: BC has a serious family doctor shortage, particularly in Metro Vancouver. Many newcomers register with the Health Connect Registry and wait months for a permanent family doctor, relying on walk-in clinics and the 8-1-1 nurse line in the meantime. The Fraser Valley and Vancouver Island generally have better family doctor access than central Vancouver.

The Brazilian community in BC

The Brazilian presence in BC has grown substantially over the past decade.

Metro Vancouver. The largest Brazilian community in Western Canada, anchored by the Brazilian Consulate in Vancouver, several Brazilian churches (Catholic and evangelical), Portuguese-speaking businesses, a few traditional restaurants, and active Facebook and WhatsApp communities. Surrey, Burnaby, and Coquitlam have particularly visible Brazilian populations.

The Fraser Valley. Abbotsford has quietly become one of the fastest-growing Brazilian communities in the province. Lower cost of living than Vancouver, a strong evangelical church network, agricultural and warehouse employers willing to sponsor work permits, and a tight-knit community where new arrivals find help quickly. This is where Up Immigration is based, and it is no accident.

Vancouver Island and the Okanagan. Smaller but growing communities, especially among healthcare workers, hospitality professionals, and remote tech workers who relocated during and after the pandemic.

For families raising bilingual kids, the Brazilian community infrastructure in BC is meaningful: weekend Portuguese schools, churches with full Brazilian congregations, and enough cultural events through the year to keep a connection alive.

Outdoor lifestyle: the actual lived reality

Most BC marketing leans heavily on mountains and ocean, and that is fair, the geography genuinely is part of daily life. A typical BC weekend can include a morning hike, an afternoon at a beach, and an evening at a craft brewery, all within an hour of Vancouver.

Year-round options:

  • Skiing and snowboarding. Whistler-Blackcomb, Cypress, Grouse, Mount Seymour all within 90 minutes of Vancouver. Big White and Silver Star near Kelowna. Mount Washington on Vancouver Island.
  • Hiking. Hundreds of trails from urban (Stanley Park, Pacific Spirit) to alpine (Garibaldi, the Chief, Joffre Lakes). Most accessible without a car if you live near transit.
  • Ocean. Kayaking, sailing, paddleboarding from Vancouver, Victoria, and the Gulf Islands. Whale watching from late spring through fall.
  • Cycling. Vancouver has one of the best urban cycling networks in North America, anchored by the Seawall (28 km of car-free waterfront path).

For newcomers from Brazil, the outdoor lifestyle is one of the most cited reasons people stay. The 67% retention rate is partly a tribute to how the weekends feel.

How BC compares to Alberta

BC and Alberta are the two most common destinations for newcomers who don't end up in Ontario or Quebec, and the choice between them is meaningful. We wrote a dedicated comparison covering tax burden, salaries, climate, and the kind of immigrant who thrives in each: BC vs Alberta: which province for newcomers.

The short version: Alberta is cheaper and pays better, BC has milder winters and a stronger immigrant community infrastructure. Both have strong PNP programs. Neither is wrong.

Is BC right for you?

BC is a strong fit if you:

  • Work in tech, healthcare, film/TV, construction, or skilled trades
  • Value mild winters more than low cost of living
  • Want to land near an established Brazilian community
  • Prioritize outdoor lifestyle and natural geography
  • Can target the BC PNP Tech, Healthcare, or International Post-Graduate streams
  • Are willing to live in the Fraser Valley, Vancouver Island, or the Okanagan rather than Vancouver itself

BC is probably the wrong fit if you:

  • Need to buy a single-detached home in Metro Vancouver on a single income
  • Hate rain and grey winters
  • Work in energy, agriculture, or industries concentrated in Alberta or the Prairies
  • Need maximum take-home pay and minimum tax burden
